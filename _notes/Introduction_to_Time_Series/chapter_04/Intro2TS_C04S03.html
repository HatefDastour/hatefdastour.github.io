4.3. Outlier Classification by Seasonality#

When working with time series data that exhibits repeating patterns—like retail sales, energy consumption, or web traffic—not all outliers are created equal. Some deviations only make sense when compared to seasonal expectations, while others represent genuine anomalies that transcend any cyclical pattern.

This classification helps you distinguish between seasonal underperformance/overperformance and true structural anomalies in your data.

Note

This section introduces seasonal vs. non-seasonal outliers and their temporal impact patterns. For detailed statistical detection methods (such as STL decomposition, ARIMA-based detection, and change-point analysis), please refer to Section XX.

4.3.1. Why Seasonality Matters#

The Core Problem:

Imagine you’re analyzing monthly retail sales. You see $1.2M in revenue for December 2024. Is that good or bad?

  • Compared to other months (e.g., March: $600K): It’s excellent—double the typical month!

  • Compared to previous Decembers (e.g., Dec 2022: \(2.8M, Dec 2023: \)3.1M): It’s terrible—significantly below seasonal expectations.

The same value can be simultaneously:

  • Normal from a global perspective

  • An outlier from a seasonal perspective

This is why we need to classify outliers based on their relationship to seasonality.

4.3.2. Seasonal Outliers (SO)#

4.3.2.1. What Are They?#

Seasonal outliers are data points that deviate significantly from the typical value for that specific seasonal period, even though they might look normal compared to other times of the year.

Key Characteristics

  • Season-Specific: Evaluated against historical data for the same season

  • Context-Dependent: A value might be higher than non-seasonal periods but still anomalous for that season

  • Relative Deviation: The anomaly is in how much it differs from seasonal norms

  • Pattern-Aware: Considers the cyclical nature of the data

Examples

  • Holiday Retail Sales

    • Normal Pattern: November-December typically see \(2M–\)3M (historical average), while the rest of the year sees \(500K–\)800K

    • Seasonal Outlier Detected: December 2024 shows $1.2M

    • Why it’s anomalous: While $1.2M is higher than typical monthly sales, it’s much lower than expected holiday sales—a significant underperformance for the season

  • Summer Ice Cream Sales

    • Normal Pattern: July typically sees 50,000 units sold (peak season), while January sees 5,000 units

    • Seasonal Outlier Detected: July 2024 shows 25,000 units

    • Why it’s anomalous: While 25,000 is far above winter sales, it’s only 50% of expected summer performance

  • Energy Consumption

    • Normal Pattern: January (winter) typically sees 150,000 kWh due to heating demand, while September sees 80,000 kWh

    • Seasonal Outlier Detected: January 2024 shows 95,000 kWh

    • Why it’s anomalous: Significantly below winter expectations, possibly indicating equipment failure or unusually mild weather

What to look for:

Fig. 4.6 shows monthly retail sales with clear seasonal peaks in November-December (holiday season). The red marker in December 2024 shows a value of $1.2M. While this is higher than most months throughout the year, it’s dramatically below the typical holiday season performance of $2M–$3M shown in previous years. This seasonal underperformance warrants investigation—perhaps marketing campaigns failed, supply chain issues occurred, or economic conditions changed.

4.3.3. Non-Seasonal Outliers#

4.3.3.1. What Are They?#

Non-seasonal outliers are anomalies that aren’t tied to any specific season or periodic pattern. They represent true, unexpected deviations from the normal flow of the time series, regardless of when they occur.

Key Characteristics

  • Season-Independent: Not related to cyclical patterns

  • Universal Deviation: Unusual regardless of time of year

  • Pure Anomaly: Represents genuine unexpected events or data issues

  • Four Temporal Impact Types: Classified by how they affect subsequent observations

4.3.4. Types of Non-Seasonal Outliers#

Non-seasonal outliers are further classified into four types based on their temporal effect patterns—how they influence the time series over time.

  • Type 1: Additive Outliers (AO) – Isolated Impact

  • Type 2: Innovative Outliers (IO) – Decaying Persistence

  • Type 3: Level Shift (LS) – Permanent Structural Change

  • Type 4: Transient Change (TC) – Temporary Structural Change

4.3.4.1. Type 1: Additive Outliers (AO) – Isolated Impact#

What Is It?

An extraordinary event that affects a single observation only. The effect is completely isolated, and the time series immediately returns to its normal pattern after the outlier. Think of it as a “one-off spike.”

Key Characteristics

  • Duration: Single time period only

  • Effect: No impact on subsequent observations

  • Recovery: Immediate return to baseline

Examples

  • Data Entry Error

    • A clerk accidentally enters \(50,000 instead of \)5,000 for a single day’s sales

    • Next day’s sales return to normal ~$5,000 range

    • No lasting impact on the business or data pattern

  • Momentary Sensor Malfunction

    • Temperature sensor briefly reads 150°C due to electrical interference

    • Next reading shows normal 22°C

    • System and actual temperature unaffected

  • Viral Social Media Spike

    • A single tweet from a celebrity drives 100,000 website visits in one day

    • Normal daily traffic: 5,000 visits

    • Next day returns to 5,000—no sustained interest

Mathematical Form

The mathematical representation of an Additive Outlier is expressed as:

(4.4)#\[\begin{equation} Y_t' = \begin{cases} Y_t + \omega_a & \text{if } t = T \\ Y_t & \text{if } t \neq T \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

Component Definitions:

  • \(Y_t'\) = The observed (contaminated) value at time \(t\) that we actually measure

  • \(Y_t\) = The true underlying value that would exist without any outlier present (the clean, uncontaminated data)

  • \(\omega_a\) = The additive magnitude or amplitude of the outlier—the fixed amount by which the observation deviates. This can be positive (upward spike) or negative (downward dip)

  • \(T\) = The specific time index where the outlier occurs (a single, fixed point in time)

  • \(t\) = Any time point in the series

What This Equation Means:

At the outlier time point \(t = T\), the observed value equals the true value plus an additive “shock” of magnitude \(\omega_a\). For all other times (\(t \neq T\)), the observed value equals the true value exactly—no contamination occurs before or after the outlier.

Example 4.1

Consider a real-world scenario tracking daily website traffic over 120 days (January to April 2024). The baseline series includes:

  • A mild upward trend (organic growth of 0.02 units per day)

  • Weekly seasonality (higher traffic on weekdays, lower on weekends)

  • Random noise representing daily fluctuations

On Day 75 (March 16, 2024), a system malfunction or data logging error occurs that causes the recorded traffic \(Y_{75}'\) to spike by exactly \(\omega_a = 12.0\) units above the true value \(Y_{75}\).

Mathematical representation:

If the true traffic on Day 75 was \(Y_{75} = 48.5\) visits (baseline), then:

  • Observed value with outlier: \(Y_{75}' = 48.5 + 12.0 = 60.5\) visits

  • Day 74 (unaffected): \(Y_{74}' = Y_{74} = 49.2\) visits

  • Day 76 (unaffected): \(Y_{76}' = Y_{76} = 50.1\) visits

The outlier only contaminates the single observation at Day 75. The day immediately before and after show normal behavior with no lasting impact. The sharp red spike in the visualization clearly illustrates this isolated disturbance—it appears suddenly, deviates significantly from the baseline (gray line), then the series resumes its normal pattern.

What to look for:

The time series shows stable baseline behavior with typical fluctuations. The sharp spike appears suddenly on one day, reaching a value far above the normal range. Critically, the day after the spike, the series immediately returns to its previous baseline level. There’s no gradual decay, no shift in the mean, no persistent effect—just an isolated blip.

4.3.4.2. Type 2: Innovative Outliers (IO) – Decaying Persistence#

What Is It?

An extraordinary event that creates a shock to the underlying system, affecting not just the current observation but also subsequent ones with gradually diminishing impact over time. Unlike an additive outlier’s isolated spike, an innovative outlier leaves a “memory” in the system that fades exponentially.

Key Characteristics

  • Duration: Affects current and multiple future observations

  • Effect: Persistent but decreasing—strongest at occurrence, then fades

  • Recovery: Gradual return to baseline over several periods

Examples

  • Successful Marketing Campaign

    • A major promotional campaign boosts sales from $50K/week baseline in Week 1

    • Week 1: $150K (initial shock from campaign launch)

    • Week 2: $90K (elevated but declining—lingering campaign effects)

    • Week 3: $70K (still above baseline—word-of-mouth continues)

    • Week 4: $55K (nearly back to normal—momentum dissipates)

    • The initial shock creates momentum that takes time to dissipate

  • Viral Content Effect

    • A viral YouTube video drives 500K website visits on Day 1 (baseline: ~10K)

    • Day 2: 200K (still very high—shares continue)

    • Day 3: 80K (declining—algorithm stops prioritizing)

    • Day 4: 30K (approaching normal)

    • Day 7: 12K (back to baseline ~10K)

    • The viral effect gradually wears off as momentum decays

  • Product Launch

    • New smartphone model creates demand shock on launch

    • Week 1: Initial rush of early adopters (baseline + 200%)

    • Week 2-4: Word-of-mouth drives continued elevated demand (declining each week)

    • Week 5-8: Demand slowly stabilizes back to baseline

    • The launch event triggers a lingering effect that gradually vanishes

Mathematical Form

The mathematical representation of an Innovative Outlier is expressed as:

(4.5)#\[\begin{equation} Y_t' = \begin{cases} Y_t + \psi_{t-T} \omega_i & \text{if } t \geq T \\ Y_t & \text{if } t < T \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

where the decay function is:

(4.6)#\[\begin{equation} \psi_{t-T} = \phi^{t-T}, \quad 0 < \phi < 1 \end{equation}\]

Component Definitions:

  • \(Y_t'\) = The observed (contaminated) value at time \(t\)

  • \(Y_t\) = The true underlying value without the outlier

  • \(\omega_i\) = The initial shock magnitude at time \(T\)—the maximum additive impact at the moment the event occurs

  • \(\psi_{t-T}\) = The decay function that controls how the shock effect diminishes over time

  • \(\phi\) = The decay factor (0 < \(\phi\) < 1), which determines how quickly the impact fades

    • \(\phi = 0.9\): Slow decay (effect lingers a long time)

    • \(\phi = 0.7\): Moderate decay (effect fades in weeks)

    • \(\phi = 0.5\): Fast decay (effect mostly gone in a few periods)

  • \(T\) = The time index when the shock event occurs

  • \(t - T\) = The number of periods since the shock (often written as \(k\))

What This Equation Means:

At time \(T\), the outlier injects a full shock of magnitude \(\omega_i\). As time progresses beyond \(T\), the shock’s influence is multiplied by the decay function \(\psi_{t-T} = \phi^{t-T}\), which shrinks exponentially. The effect never completely disappears mathematically, but becomes negligible after several periods.

For example, with \(\phi = 0.85\):

  • At \(t = T\) (Day 0): Impact = \(0.85^0 \times \omega_i = 1.0 \times \omega_i\) (full shock)

  • At \(t = T+1\) (Day 1): Impact = \(0.85^1 \times \omega_i = 0.85 \times \omega_i\) (85% remaining)

  • At \(t = T+2\) (Day 2): Impact = \(0.85^2 \times \omega_i = 0.72 \times \omega_i\) (72% remaining)

  • At \(t = T+5\) (Day 5): Impact = \(0.85^5 \times \omega_i = 0.44 \times \omega_i\) (44% remaining)

  • At \(t = T+10\) (Day 10): Impact = \(0.85^{10} \times \omega_i = 0.20 \times \omega_i\) (20% remaining)

Example 4.2

Consider a real-world scenario tracking daily website traffic over 150 days (January to May 2024). The baseline series includes:

  • A mild upward trend (organic growth of 0.015 units per day)

  • Weekly seasonality (higher traffic on weekdays, lower on weekends)

  • Random noise representing daily fluctuations

On Day 70 (March 11, 2024), a successful viral marketing campaign launches that creates an initial shock of \(\omega_i = 10.0\) additional visits. However, unlike a data entry error (which affects only one day), this campaign’s effect decays gradually with decay factor \(\phi = 0.85\).

Mathematical representation:

If the baseline traffic on Day 70 was \(Y_{70} = 62.5\) visits, then:

  • Day 70 (shock event at T): \(Y_{70}' = 62.5 + (0.85^0 \times 10.0) = 62.5 + 10.0 = 72.5\) visits

  • Day 71 (one day later): \(Y_{71}' = Y_{71} + (0.85^1 \times 10.0) = Y_{71} + 8.5\) visits

  • Day 72: \(Y_{72}' = Y_{72} + (0.85^2 \times 10.0) = Y_{72} + 7.2\) visits

  • Day 75 (5 days later): \(Y_{75}' = Y_{75} + (0.85^5 \times 10.0) = Y_{75} + 4.4\) visits

  • Day 80 (10 days later): \(Y_{80}' = Y_{80} + (0.85^{10} \times 10.0) = Y_{80} + 2.0\) visits

Key observation: The outlier’s effect doesn’t vanish after one day like an additive outlier. Instead, it persists and gradually decays—each day the additional impact is 85% of the previous day’s impact. After about 10 days, the campaign’s effect has diminished to just 20% of its original shock value, but it continues to fade slowly.

Fig. 4.9 shows the difference between baseline behavior (gray line) and the observed data with the innovative outlier (blue line). The sharp upward shift begins at Day 70 when the campaign launches. Rather than returning to baseline the next day, the series gradually descends back toward the baseline in an exponential decay pattern. The orange shaded area between the two lines represents the persistent impact zone—notice how its width shrinks as days pass, illustrating the decaying nature of the effect.

What to look for:

The initial shock appears as a dramatic spike above baseline. However, unlike an additive outlier, the series doesn’t immediately return to the previous level. Instead, you see a gradual decay pattern—each subsequent observation is closer to the baseline than the previous one, creating an exponential decay curve back to normal. The system has been “perturbed” and takes time to settle. The gap between the observed and baseline values shrinks in a smooth, mathematically predictable way rather than dropping sharply.

4.3.4.3. Type 3: Level Shift (LS) – Permanent Structural Change#

What Is It?

An abrupt and permanent change in the mean level of the time series. After the shift occurs, the series stabilizes at a new baseline that persists indefinitely. Unlike an innovative outlier that decays, a level shift represents a structural break where the underlying system itself has changed.

Key Characteristics

  • Duration: Permanent, indefinite persistence

  • Effect: Constant long-term impact on series level

  • Recovery: No recovery—the change is permanent

  • Pattern: Step function—immediate jump to new mean level

Examples

  • Retail Store Expansion

    • Company operates at baseline revenue of $500K/month

    • After opening 10 new store locations, immediate jump occurs

    • Post-expansion: $1.2M/month (permanent new baseline)

    • Revenue remains elevated indefinitely—no decay back to original level

    • The structural change in business capacity creates a permanent revenue increase

  • New Government Regulation

    • Environmental tax implemented on manufacturing

    • Pre-tax production cost: $80/unit (baseline)

    • Post-tax implementation: $95/unit (permanent new cost)

    • Production costs remain elevated indefinitely due to policy change

    • Once the tax is in place, costs don’t gradually return to previous levels

  • Company Merger

    • Company A (revenue: \(5M/quarter) merges with Company B (revenue: \)3M/quarter)

    • Pre-merger: $5M/quarter baseline

    • Post-merger: $8M/quarter (new permanent baseline)

    • Combined entity operates at structurally higher revenue level indefinitely

  • Technology Implementation

    • Factory implements automation system, reducing production time per unit

    • Pre-automation baseline: 100 units/day

    • Post-automation: 250 units/day (permanent efficiency gain)

    • Operational capacity remains elevated—the automation doesn’t wear off

    • Productivity increase is structural, not temporary

Mathematical Form

The mathematical representation of a Level Shift is expressed as:

(4.7)#\[\begin{equation} Y_t' = \begin{cases} Y_t & \text{if } t < T \\ Y_t + \omega_{ls} & \text{if } t \geq T \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

Component Definitions:

  • \(Y_t'\) = The observed value at time \(t\)

  • \(Y_t\) = The true underlying value without the shift

  • \(\omega_{ls}\) = The level shift magnitude—the permanent, fixed amount by which the entire series level changes. This remains constant for all \(t \geq T\)

  • \(T\) = The time index when the structural shift occurs (the change point)

  • \(t\) = Any time point in the series

What This Equation Means:

Before the shift time \(t = T\), the observed value equals the true value (no contamination). At \(t = T\) and for all subsequent times \(t > T\), the observed value equals the true value plus a constant, permanent additive amount \(\omega_{ls}\). This shift never decays, never changes—it’s a permanent structural alteration to the system’s baseline level.

The key distinction from other outlier types:

  • Additive Outlier (AO): Affects only \(t = T\), then disappears

  • Innovative Outlier (IO): Affects \(t \geq T\) but decays exponentially over time

  • Level Shift (LS): Affects \(t \geq T\) with constant magnitude forever—no decay

For example, with a shift magnitude \(\omega_{ls} = 8.0\) occurring at \(T = 100\):

  • At \(t = 99\) (before shift): \(Y_{99}' = Y_{99}\) (no offset)

  • At \(t = 100\) (shift occurs): \(Y_{100}' = Y_{100} + 8.0\) (offset appears)

  • At \(t = 101\): \(Y_{101}' = Y_{101} + 8.0\) (same offset persists)

  • At \(t = 200\): \(Y_{200}' = Y_{200} + 8.0\) (offset unchanged, even 100 periods later)

Example 4.3

Consider a real-world scenario tracking daily production output over 200 days (January to July 2024). The baseline series includes:

  • A small upward trend (organic efficiency gains of 0.01 units/day)

  • Weekly seasonality (lower output on Mondays, higher on Thursdays)

  • Random noise representing daily operational variability

On Day 100 (April 10, 2024), the company implements a new automation system that permanently increases production capacity. This creates a level shift of \(\omega_{ls} = 8.0\) units—production immediately jumps and stays at this elevated level.

Mathematical representation:

If the baseline production on Day 100 was \(Y_{100} = 82.5\) units, then:

  • Day 99 (before automation): \(Y_{99}' = Y_{99} = 81.2\) units (no shift)

  • Day 100 (automation implemented at T): \(Y_{100}' = 82.5 + 8.0 = 90.5\) units (shift occurs)

  • Day 101 (one day later): \(Y_{101}' = Y_{101} + 8.0\) units (offset persists)

  • Day 150 (50 days later): \(Y_{150}' = Y_{150} + 8.0\) units (still elevated by same amount)

  • Day 200 (100 days later): \(Y_{200}' = Y_{200} + 8.0\) units (offset remains unchanged)

Key observation: Unlike an innovative outlier that decays back toward the original baseline, or an additive outlier that affects only a single day, this level shift permanently alters the series level. The production output remains 8.0 units higher every single day for the rest of the observation period—and would continue indefinitely. There is no “recovery” to the original level; the structural change is permanent.

Fig. 4.10 clearly shows the difference between baseline behavior (gray line) and the observed data with the level shift (blue line). Before Day 100, both lines fluctuate around the same baseline level. At Day 100 (marked by the vertical red dashed line), the observed series immediately jumps upward. Critically, after the jump, the series does not drift back downward—instead, it continues to fluctuate around a permanently elevated mean level (shown by the green dashed line). The orange dashed line represents the pre-shift mean, illustrating how the mean has permanently increased. The constant vertical distance between baseline and observed post-shift demonstrates the persistent, unchanging nature of \(\omega_{ls}\).

What to look for:

Before the change point, the series fluctuates around one stable baseline level. At a specific change point, there’s a sharp, immediate jump. After the change point, the series fluctuates around a new, higher (or lower) baseline with no tendency to return to the previous level. The “before” and “after” sections look like two separate time series at different mean levels. Unlike a decaying outlier where the gap shrinks over time, the gap between pre-shift and post-shift levels remains constant—the shift is truly structural and permanent.

4.3.4.4. Type 4: Transient Change (TC) – Temporary Structural Change#

What Is It?

A temporary structural change similar to a level shift, but its effect gradually decays over time rather than being permanent. The series initially experiences a shock or shift, then slowly drifts back to its original baseline. Think of it as a “temporary new normal” that eventually fades away.

Key Characteristics

  • Duration: Temporary with exponential decay

  • Effect: Initial impact resembles a level shift, then gradual return

  • Recovery: Exponential decay back to baseline

  • Pattern: Step change, then smooth exponential decay downward

Examples

  • Labor Strike

    • Factory operates at baseline: 1,000 units/day

    • Strike occurs—immediate drop to 200 units/day

    • Strike ends but productivity doesn’t immediately recover

    • Week 1 post-strike: 400 units/day (still impaired)

    • Week 2: 600 units/day (improving)

    • Week 4: 900 units/day (nearly recovered)

    • Week 6: 1,000 units/day (full recovery)

    • The impact decays gradually as production ramping accelerates

  • Supply Chain Disruption

    • Hurricane damages supplier facility, forcing expensive alternative suppliers

    • Unit cost jumps from \(50 to \)80 (immediate impact)

    • As supplier rebuilds and resumes production:

      • Month 1: $75/unit (partial recovery)

      • Month 2: $62/unit (improving)

      • Month 3: $52/unit (nearly back)

      • Month 4: $50/unit (fully recovered)

    • The cost advantage from the normal supplier gradually returns

  • Seasonal Marketing Campaign

    • Major holiday advertising campaign boosts sales permanently

    • Campaign ends, but brand awareness and momentum linger

    • Sales remain elevated for several weeks post-campaign

    • Gradually decay back to baseline as brand recall fades naturally

    • Unlike permanent level shifts, the effect is truly temporary

  • Weather Event Impact

    • Severe winter storm disrupts retail operations

    • Sales drop 60% during storm week (initial shock)

    • Recovery is gradual as store repairs, restocking, and customer confidence return

    • Week 1 post-storm: 50% of baseline (still recovering)

    • Week 2: 75% of baseline (improving)

    • Week 4: 95% of baseline (nearly normal)

    • Takes 3-4 weeks to fully return to normal sales patterns

Mathematical Form

The mathematical representation of a Transient Change is expressed as:

(4.8)#\[\begin{equation} Y_t' = \begin{cases} Y_t & \text{if } t < T \\ Y_t + \delta^{t-T} \cdot \text{impact} & \text{if } t \geq T \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

where the decay function is:

(4.9)#\[\begin{equation} \psi_{t-T} = \delta^{t-T}, \quad 0 < \delta < 1 \end{equation}\]

Component Definitions:

  • \(Y_t'\) = The observed value at time \(t\)

  • \(Y_t\) = The true underlying value without the transient change

  • \(\text{impact}_0\) = The initial magnitude of the transient change at \(t = T\)—the immediate shock size

  • \(\psi_{t-T}\) = The decay function that controls how the transient effect diminishes

  • \(\delta\) = The decay factor (0 < \(\delta\) < 1), which determines the rate of recovery

    • \(\delta = 0.95\): Very slow decay (effect lingers for months)

    • \(\delta = 0.80\): Moderate decay (effect fades over weeks)

    • \(\delta = 0.60\): Faster decay (effect largely gone in 10-15 days)

    • \(\delta = 0.30\): Very fast decay (effect nearly gone in a few days)

  • \(T\) = The time index when the transient event occurs (the onset point)

  • \(t - T\) = The number of periods since the transient change began

What This Equation Means:

Before time \(T\), the observed value equals the true value (no disturbance). At \(t = T\), the transient change begins with full magnitude \(\text{impact}_0\). Unlike a level shift that remains constant, the transient effect decays exponentially through the function \(\psi_{t-T} = \delta^{t-T}\). As time progresses, the observed value gradually approaches the baseline again. Mathematically, the effect approaches zero asymptotically but never quite disappears.

Comparison with Other Outlier Types:

  • Additive Outlier (AO): \(Y_T' = Y_T + \omega_a\), then \(Y_{T+1}' = Y_{T+1}\) (disappears immediately)

  • Innovative Outlier (IO): Similar decay form as TC, but the decay begins from the shock point itself

  • Level Shift (LS): \(Y_t' = Y_t + \omega_{ls}\) for all \(t \geq T\) (constant, no decay)

  • Transient Change (TC): Combines level shift’s initial step with IO’s exponential decay (temporary structural change)

For example, with initial impact \(\text{impact}_0 = 9.0\) and decay factor \(\delta = 0.80\) occurring at \(T = 60\):

  • At \(t = 60\) (onset): Impact = \(0.80^0 \times 9.0 = 9.0\) (full initial impact)

  • At \(t = 61\): Impact = \(0.80^1 \times 9.0 = 7.2\) (80% of initial)

  • At \(t = 62\): Impact = \(0.80^2 \times 9.0 = 5.76\) (64% of initial)

  • At \(t = 65\): Impact = \(0.80^5 \times 9.0 = 2.95\) (33% of initial)

  • At \(t = 75\): Impact = \(0.80^{15} \times 9.0 = 0.34\) (4% of initial)

Example 4.4

Consider a real-world scenario tracking daily retail sales over 180 days (January to June 2024). The baseline series includes:

  • A small upward trend (seasonal growth of 0.012 units/day)

  • Weekly seasonality (higher sales on weekends, lower on Mondays)

  • Random noise representing daily customer variability

On Day 60 (March 1, 2024), a major supply chain disruption occurs—a hurricane damages the primary supplier facility. This forces the company to use more expensive alternative suppliers, creating an initial shock of \(\text{impact}_0 = 9.0\) in daily unit costs. However, as the primary supplier rebuilds and resumes operations, the disruption’s effect gradually decays with factor \(\delta = 0.80\).

Mathematical representation:

If the baseline cost per unit on Day 60 was \(Y_{60} = 70.0\), then:

  • Day 59 (before disruption): \(Y_{59}' = Y_{59} = 70.0\) (no change)

  • Day 60 (disruption onset at T): \(Y_{60}' = 70.0 + (0.80^0 \times 9.0) = 70.0 + 9.0 = 79.0\) (full impact)

  • Day 61 (one day later): \(Y_{61}' = Y_{61} + (0.80^1 \times 9.0) = Y_{61} + 7.2\) (80% of impact remains)

  • Day 62: \(Y_{62}' = Y_{62} + (0.80^2 \times 9.0) = Y_{62} + 5.76\) (64% of impact)

  • Day 65 (5 days later): \(Y_{65}' = Y_{65} + (0.80^5 \times 9.0) = Y_{65} + 2.95\) (only 33% remains)

  • Day 75 (15 days later): \(Y_{75}' = Y_{75} + (0.80^{15} \times 9.0) = Y_{75} + 0.34\) (only 4% remains)

Key observation: The transient change creates an initial spike that resembles a level shift—costs jump from 70 to 79. However, unlike a permanent level shift, the excess cost gradually declines. By Day 75 (two weeks after the event), the disruption’s effect has diminished to nearly nothing, and the series has recovered most of the way back to its original baseline. The recovery follows an exponential decay pattern rather than remaining stuck at the elevated level or returning immediately.

Fig. 4.11 shows the behavior of a transient change. Before Day 60 (marked by the vertical red dashed line), the baseline (gray line) and observed series (blue line) are identical. At Day 60, the observed series jumps upward due to the disruption—resembling a level shift. However, critically, the series does not stay at this elevated level. Instead, the orange shaded area between the baseline and observed lines gradually shrinks, indicating the decaying nature of the transient effect. The observed series curves back toward the baseline in an exponential decay pattern, eventually approaching it asymptotically.

What to look for:

The series shows a sudden step change (upward or downward) similar to a level shift. However, unlike a level shift that permanently stays at the new level, you observe a gradual drift back toward the original baseline. The recovery isn’t immediate like an additive outlier, and it’s not a continuous decay from the shock point like an innovative outlier. Instead, the series appears to “step” to a new level momentarily, then slowly recover from that temporarily elevated (or depressed) state. The gap between observed and baseline systematically shrinks over time in an exponential decay pattern.

4.3.6. Why Should You Care?#

You might be wondering, “Why does it matter what we call them? Can’t I just flag them all as anomalies?”

Actually, the classification changes everything about how you handle them.

4.3.6.1. 1. It Changes the “Fix”#

If you treat a Level Shift (like opening a new store) as a simple Additive Outlier (like a data error), your model will spend the next six months thinking your data is “wrong” because it’s higher than it used to be.

  • For Glitches (AO): Just delete or smooth over the bad point.

  • For New Normals (LS): Don’t delete it! You need to update your model to accept this as the new reality.

4.3.6.2. 2. It Tells You Where to Look#

The shape of the outlier is a clue to its root cause.

  • Is it a sharp spike? (AO) Check for sensor failures, data entry typos, or one-day events.

  • Is it a permanent jump? (LS) Look for major structural changes—did you acquire a company? Change a law? Launch a new website?

  • Is it fading out? (IO/TC) Look for external shocks like a PR crisis, a marketing splash, or a supply shortage that is slowly resolving.

4.3.6.3. 3. It Saves Your Forecasts#

Imagine you had a massive sales spike last month.

  • If it was a Level Shift, you should predict higher sales for all of next year.

  • If it was just an Additive Outlier, you should ignore it and predict normal sales.

  • Get this wrong, and your budget for next year will be wildly inaccurate.

4.3.7. Key Takeaways#

If you remember nothing else from this section, remember this:

  1. Context is King: You can’t call a number an “outlier” without looking at the season. Selling 1,000 units in December might be a disaster, while selling 1,000 in July is a miracle.

  2. Look at the Aftermath: Don’t just look at the spike; look at what happens after the spike. Does it stay high? Does it snap back? That behavior tells you exactly what kind of outlier you’re dealing with.

  3. Classify Before You Clean: Don’t blindly remove outliers. If you delete a Level Shift, you’re deleting the truth.
