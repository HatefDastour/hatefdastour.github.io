1.1. What are Time Series?#

1.1.1. What are Time Series?#

A time series is a sequence of data points indexed in chronological order. Most commonly, these points are recorded at regular intervals, forming a discrete-time sequence. Each observation represents the state of a variable (or variables) at a specific moment, making the temporal order an essential part of the data’s meaning.

Time series differ from other data types in that reordering the observations changes their interpretation. Measurements close in time are often more related than those farther apart, a property known as temporal dependence or autocorrelation. In many modern applications, time series arrive as continuous data streams from sensors, instruments, or digital services, requiring specialized methods for storage, processing, and analysis. [Time series, 2025].

Example 1.1 (Microsoft)

Some of the most recognizable time series data include stock prices fluctuating over trading days, temperature measurements recorded hourly or daily, GDP growth rates measured quarterly, and heart rate monitoring through medical devices. The example below visualizes Microsoft’s (MSFT) closing stock prices over the last year—a modern illustration of time series in financial markets, showing both daily volatility and evolving market trends.

Time series data has several key characteristics that distinguish it from other types of data:

  1. Temporal Ordering: Time series data has a natural temporal ordering that makes it distinct from cross-sectional studies where there is no natural ordering of observations. The timestamp associated with each data record serves as the key for computing or analysis. This ordering is fundamental—changing the sequence of observations fundamentally changes the nature of the data and the insights that can be drawn from it. Example: In climate monitoring, the sequence of daily temperature readings matters; if the order is shuffled, trends such as seasonal warming or cooling cannot be identified.

  2. Sequential Nature: Observations close together in time tend to be more closely related than observations further apart. This temporal dependency is a fundamental property that influences analysis methods. For instance, today’s stock price is more likely to be similar to yesterday’s price than to the price from a year ago. This property, known as temporal autocorrelation, underlies many time series forecasting models. Example: In hydrology, river discharge on consecutive days is often correlated because upstream water levels change gradually over time.

  3. Stream-like Properties: Time series data from connected devices can be thought of as continuous data streams, flowing into databases with high write/read ratios and immutable characteristics. Modern IoT deployments generate millions of sensor readings per second, creating unprecedented volumes of time series data that require specialized storage and processing approaches. Example: Smart electricity meters send second-by-second household energy consumption data to utility providers for real-time load balancing.

1.1.2. Time Series Formats#

Time series data extends far beyond classical tables—though numeric, timestamped records remain the most familiar form. At their core, time series are unified by their temporal sequence, but their modality and structure shape the required storage, analysis, and preprocessing methods.

Below is a unified, academic overview of the main time series formats, enhanced with:

  • Discussion of their analytical/research implications

  • Notes on preprocessing and storage

  • Common software tools for each modality

  • Current advances including foundation models and unified AI approaches

1.1.2.1. Traditional Numeric Series#

Definition: Tabular records associating each timestamp with one or more numeric measurements—used in finance, economics, science, and industrial monitoring.

  • Univariate (single variable): Each series tracks a single variable (e.g., a stock’s daily closing price).

    • Classical analysis: ARIMA or exponential smoothing for forecasting; trend and seasonality extraction.

    • Modern approaches: Foundation models (TimesFM, Chronos) for zero-shot forecasting; transformer-based architectures for long-term predictions.

    • Preprocessing: Outlier detection, missing value imputation, resampling for consistent intervals.

    • Software tools: R (forecast, tsibble, fable), Python (pandas, statsmodels, prophet, sktime, darts).

  • Multivariate (multiple related variables): Each record contains several tied measurements (e.g., temperature, humidity, wind speed measured at every hour).

    • Classical analysis: Vector autoregression (VAR), multivariate state-space models, anomaly detection.

    • Modern approaches: Transformer-based multivariate forecasting, foundation models with cross-series attention mechanisms.

    • Example dataset: Air Quality Dataset (hourly readings of pollutants and weather parameters).

  • Panel or multiple independent series: Multiple separate but structurally similar series (e.g., rainfall in several unrelated cities or sales across many stores).

    • Classical analysis: Panel data regression, mixed-effects modeling, cross-sectional comparison.

    • Modern approaches: Foundation models trained on heterogeneous panels for universal forecasting across domains.

    • Example dataset: FRED-QD (quarterly economic data for multiple U.S. regions).

  • Storage formats:

    • Wide format: Each time series/variable in its own column (easy for visualization).

    • Long format: Stacked format with series/variable IDs; more flexible for modeling and aggregation.

  • Storage considerations: For high-frequency, long-span data, time series databases like InfluxDB or TimescaleDB are often used. Columnar file formats (e.g., Apache Parquet, Arrow) are efficient for analytics workloads.

1.1.2.2. Event-Driven or Irregular Time Series#

Definition: Time-stamped data captured only when triggering events occur, as in logs or alerts. Intervals between entries vary.

  • Example: Security system records motion detection times; server logs capture only errors or significant changes.

  • Classical analysis: Point process modeling, event clustering, survival analysis.

  • Modern approaches: Neural point processes, transformer-based event sequence modeling, foundation models for irregular time series.

  • Preprocessing: Temporal normalization, deduplication, event aggregation by interval.

  • Event streaming tools: For real-time, at-scale applications, platforms like Apache Kafka and Pulsar manage and distribute these streams.

1.1.2.3. Signal-Oriented Data (Audio, Biomedical, Sensor)#

Definition: Continuous or discrete signal measurements over time, often requiring both time- and frequency-domain analysis.

  • Audio/speech: Waveform of speech/audio for recognition, speaker identification.

    • Classical tools: Python (scipy.signal), MATLAB (Signal Processing Toolbox).

    • Modern approaches: Transformer-based audio processing, foundation models for speech and audio understanding.

  • Biomedical signals: ECG, EEG; stored in EDF, WFDB formats.

    • Tools: physiozoo.com for PPG (& benchmarks), pySPACE for EEG analysis.

    • Modern approaches: Deep learning for automated diagnosis, foundation models for multi-modal biomedical signal analysis.

  • Sensor/IoT streams: Temperature, vibration, and industrial metrics sampled at high frequencies; increasingly stored in TsFile, TSDF, or columnar formats for durability and efficiency.

    • Preprocessing: Filtering, resampling, artifact removal.

    • Modern approaches: Edge AI for real-time processing, foundation models for predictive maintenance.

1.1.2.4. Image and Video Time Series#

Definition: Sequences of images or video frames indexed by time—common in geospatial (earth observation), scientific, and medical contexts.

  • Examples:

    • Satellite scene series (e.g., LandSat images over a city across decades).

    • MRI scan progression for patient monitoring.

    • Security cameras, wildlife monitoring.

  • Storage: Specialized multimedia containers (combine audio, video, annotations), but for analytical workflows, image arrays are often stored as 3D+ arrays (e.g., [time, height, width, channels]).

  • Classical tools: scikit-image and OpenCV (Python); VANTAGE for synchronizing video and time-series bio-logger data; SamuROI for multi-scale optical imaging in neuroscience.

  • Modern approaches: Vision transformers for video analysis, foundation models combining computer vision and time series modeling, multi-modal architectures processing synchronized video and sensor data.

1.1.2.5. Event Camera Streams#

Definition: Ultra-fast vision sensors produce streams of asynchronous pixel-level events (not full frames) every time brightness changes—rather than at fixed intervals.

  • Example: Recording high-speed robotic motion or ball trajectories with minimal blur and low power.

  • Classical analysis: Specialized event-based vision algorithms; typical storage as event log files with pixel coordinates, time, and polarity.

  • Modern approaches: Neuromorphic computing architectures, spiking neural networks, event-based transformer models.

1.1.2.6. Textual and Log Streams#

Definition: Sequences of time-stamped textual entries, such as structured system logs, chat transcripts, or social media posts.

  • Examples:

    • Twitter traffic during news events.

    • Server/application logs for anomaly detection.

    • IoT text logs from connected sensors.

  • Classical analysis: Trend detection, text mining, event correlation.

  • Modern approaches: Large language models for log analysis, foundation models combining NLP and time series modeling for contextual understanding.

  • Preprocessing: Parsing, timestamp alignment, log template discovery, text normalization.

  • Tools: Python’s pandas for time series logs; nltk/spaCy for text; log-specific tools for organizing, searching, and visualizing event logs.

1.1.3. Structural and Analytical Perspectives#

  • Metrics vs. Events

    • Metrics: Regularly sampled (e.g., hourly weather readings).

    • Events: Irregular, trigger-driven (e.g., system fault alerts).

  • Classical vs. Modern Modeling

    • Classical: Suitable for interpretable, statistical approaches (ARIMA, linear regression, STL decomposition).

    • Foundation Models: Large-scale pre-trained architectures providing zero-shot and few-shot capabilities across diverse tasks and domains.

    • Hybrid Approaches: Combining classical statistical methods with modern AI for enhanced performance and interpretability.

1.1.4. Storage and Preprocessing Notes#

  • Time Series Databases: InfluxDB and TimescaleDB are optimized for time-indexed ingestion/querying; TimescaleDB excels with multi-metric analytics, while InfluxDB is often used for sensor/IoT scale.

  • Columnar Data Formats: Parquet and Arrow allow fast retrieval, storage efficiency, and seamless Python/R integration, especially for analytic workloads.

  • Event Streaming Platforms: Apache Kafka and similar systems enable scalable, real-time ingestion and processing of event-driven or high-velocity time series.

  • Preprocessing Considerations: Each modality may require different steps—e.g., imputation of missing values for numeric data, filtering and noise reduction for signals, or parsing and normalization for text streams.

  • Foundation Model Integration: Modern workflows increasingly incorporate pre-trained foundation models that can handle multiple data modalities and tasks with minimal preprocessing.

1.1.5. Current State of the Art: Foundation Models and Unified Approaches#

The field has recently witnessed transformative advances with foundation models—large, pre-trained architectures that can handle diverse time series tasks:

Key Developments:

  • Universal Models: Foundation models like TimesFM, TimeGPT, Chronos, and TimesBERT provide zero-shot forecasting, anomaly detection, and classification across domains without task-specific training.

  • Multi-modal Integration: Current architectures seamlessly combine numeric time series with text, images, and event data for contextual understanding.

  • Synthetic Data Augmentation: Advanced synthetic data generation techniques enhance model training and enable privacy-preserving applications.

  • Hybrid Excellence: State-of-the-art systems often combine classical statistical methods with foundation models, leveraging the interpretability of traditional approaches with the power of modern AI.

Practical Impact: Students and practitioners should become familiar with both classical time series analysis (for interpretability and domain-specific insights) and modern foundation model APIs and frameworks (for scalable, generalizable solutions).
