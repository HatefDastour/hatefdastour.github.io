1.3. Key Concepts in Geospatial Data#

1.3.1. Spatial Frameworks#

At the heart of any GIS is the spatial framework, which is essentially the scaffolding that supports all subsequent layers of geospatial data. It’s the foundation upon which we build our understanding of geographic spaces and their relationships. A spatial framework takes real-world locations and translates them into a format that can be easily managed and analyzed within a GIS.

Key Components of Spatial Frameworks:

  1. Spatial Reference Systems (SRS): These systems provide a set of rules for how points on the Earth’s surface are mapped to coordinates. They ensure that spatial data from different sources can be integrated and compared in a meaningful way.

    Example:: The World Geodetic System 1984 (WGS 84) is a global reference system for geospatial information and is the reference coordinate system used by the Global Positioning System (GPS).

    Example:: EPSG:3401 refers to a specific projected coordinate system used for spatial data management in Canada, particularly in Alberta. It’s part of the EPSG (European Petroleum Survey Group) Geodetic Parameter Dataset, which is a collection of definitions of coordinate reference systems and coordinate transformations.

    • Coordinate System: NAD83 / Alberta 10-TM (Resource)

    • Area of Use: Canada - Alberta

    • Details: This system uses the North American Datum of 1983 (NAD83) and applies a Transverse Mercator projection tailored for the 10th meridian west in Alberta. It’s designed for resource management and has negative easting coordinates in western Alberta, which means the coordinate values west of the central meridian are negative.

    For an alternative system with positive easting coordinates, the NAD83 / Alberta 10-TM (Forest) with the code “EPSG:3400” can be used. The choice between these systems depends on the specific requirements of the spatial data application in use.

  2. Geodetic Networks: These are networks of physical reference points on the Earth’s surface that provide accurate, measured locations that serve as the basis for mapping and surveying.

    Example:: The National Spatial Reference System (NSRS) in the United States is a network of over 300,000 geodetic points that are used for mapping and surveying across the United States.

  3. Grid Systems: Virtual grids overlaid on the Earth’s surface help in referencing spatial data. They can be global, like the lines of latitude and longitude, or local, like the grids used for city planning.

    Example:: The Universal Transverse Mercator (UTM) is a type of grid system that divides the world into a series of 6-degree longitudinal zones for detailed mapping and navigation.

  4. Spatial Data Models: These models define how spatial data is represented within the GIS. They can be vector-based, representing discrete objects like roads and buildings, or raster-based, representing continuous data like elevation or temperature.

    Example:: A vector data model might represent a road network in a city, while a raster data model could represent the varying elevations across a mountain range.

The evolution of GIS technology has expanded the capabilities of spatial frameworks. Modern GIS now integrates desktop tools with web-based services, allowing for more dynamic and accessible spatial analysis. This shift towards a web-centric paradigm means that spatial frameworks must be adaptable to both traditional desktop GIS environments and modern web-based platforms.

1.3.2. Understanding Coordinate Systems in GIS#

Coordinate systems are the scaffolding that allows us to define the position of features in two- or three-dimensional space. They are essential for locating and displaying data correctly on the Earth’s surface and relative to one another.

1.3.2.1. Types of Coordinate Systems#

  1. Geographic Coordinate Systems (GCS): These are based on a three-dimensional ellipsoidal or spherical surface. Locations are defined using angular measurements, usually in decimal degrees, measuring degrees of longitude (x-coordinates) and degrees of latitude (y-coordinates).

  2. Projected Coordinate Systems (PCS): These are planar systems that use linear measurements for coordinates rather than angular units. A PCS is composed of a GCS and a map projection. The map projection contains the mathematical calculations that convert the angular coordinates of the GCS to Cartesian coordinates of the PCS.

  3. Local Coordinate Systems: These are often used for engineering projects or other applications requiring high precision over a smaller area. They are tailored to specific locations and may not be compatible with global or regional coordinate systems.

1.3.2.2. Choosing the Right Coordinate System#

Selecting an appropriate coordinate system is crucial for the accuracy and usability of spatial data. The choice depends on the scale of the project, the region of interest, and the intended use of the data. For instance, a global study might use a GCS like WGS84, while a city map might use a PCS that minimizes distortion in that area.

1.3.2.3. Horizontal and Vertical Coordinate Systems#

  • Horizontal Coordinate Systems: Define where features are across the globe.

  • Vertical Coordinate Systems: Define how high or deep features are relative to a surface.

1.3.2.4. Importance of Coordinate Systems in GIS#

Coordinate systems are vital for various GIS operations, including data integration, map creation, and spatial analysis. They ensure that spatial data from different sources can be compared and analyzed in a consistent manner. Without a common coordinate system, it would be challenging to overlay maps, compare spatial data, or conduct accurate spatial analyses.

1.3.3. Datum#

A datum is essentially a mathematical model of the Earth that serves as a foundational reference for spatial data. It defines the position of the spheroid relative to the center of the Earth and establishes the origin and orientation of latitude and longitude lines.

1.3.3.1. The Role of a Datum in GIS#

  • Reference Frame: A datum provides a frame of reference for measuring locations on the Earth’s surface, ensuring that spatial data is consistent and comparable.

  • Shape of the Earth: It includes a model of the Earth’s shape, which is necessary because the Earth is not a perfect sphere but an irregularly shaped ellipsoid.

1.3.3.2. Types of Datums#

  1. Geocentric Datums: These use the Earth’s center of mass as the origin, such as the widely used World Geodetic System of 1984 (WGS84). They relate coordinates to the Earth’s center of mass and are essential for global applications like GPS.

  2. Local Datums: These align their spheroid to fit the Earth’s surface in a specific area. An example is the North American Datum of 1927 (NAD27), which was designed to fit North America reasonably well.

1.3.3.3. Datum Transformation#

When you change the datum, the coordinate values of your data will change. This is because different datums have different origins and measurements. For example, the coordinates of a point in Redlands, California, differ slightly when measured using NAD83 compared to NAD27 due to the different reference frames and spheroid models used.

1.3.3.4. Importance of Datum in GIS#

The choice of datum can significantly affect the accuracy of spatial analyses. It is crucial for tasks such as surveying, mapping, and navigation. A correct datum ensures that spatial data aligns with the physical world, which is vital for any geospatial project.

1.3.4. Map Projections#

A map projection is a systematic method to translate the Earth’s spherical surface onto a flat map. This process involves mathematical calculations that transform the angular coordinates of latitude and longitude into planar, two-dimensional coordinates.

1.3.4.1. Why Map Projections are Necessary#

  • Globes vs. Maps: While a globe can accurately represent the Earth, it’s not practical for many purposes. Maps, on the other hand, are easy to use and transport but require a projection to represent the Earth’s surface.

  • Distortion: Any map projection introduces some level of distortion because it’s impossible to flatten a sphere without stretching or compressing its surface. Different projections handle these distortions in various ways.

1.3.4.2. Types of Map Projections#

  1. Cylindrical Projections: These wrap a cylinder around the globe. The Mercator projection is a well-known example that preserves angles, making it useful for navigation despite distorting areas, especially near the poles.

  2. Conic Projections: These involve projecting the Earth’s surface onto a cone. They are particularly suited for mapping mid-latitude regions where they maintain a balance between shape and area distortion.

  3. Azimuthal Projections: These project the Earth’s surface onto a plane, typically touching the globe at a single point. They are often used for polar regions and can preserve either distances or directions from the center point.

1.3.4.3. Choosing the Right Projection#

The choice of a map projection depends on the purpose of the map:

  • Preserving Area: Equal-area projections, like the Albers Equal Area, maintain the relative size of areas across the map.

  • Preserving Shape: Conformal projections, like the Lambert Conformal Conic, maintain the shape of small areas.

  • Preserving Distance: Equidistant projections maintain accurate distances from certain points or along certain lines.

  • Preserving Direction: Azimuthal projections can maintain accurate directions from the central point to any other point on the map.

1.3.4.4. The Impact of Projections#

The selection of a map projection can influence how we perceive spatial relationships and can have implications for everything from navigation to political perceptions. For example, the size distortion of the Mercator projection has been criticized for giving an inflated sense of the size of higher-latitude countries.

1.3.5. Importance of Geospatial Data#

Geospatial data finds application in a wide range of fields:

  • Urban Planning: City planners use geospatial data to design efficient transportation systems, plan zoning, and manage public utilities.

  • Environmental Conservation: Conservationists employ geospatial data to monitor habitats, track wildlife, and evaluate the impacts of climate change.

  • Public Safety: Emergency responders utilize geospatial data for optimizing routes, managing disasters, and allocating resources.

  • Business Intelligence: Companies harness geospatial data for market analysis, logistics, and strategic planning.

1.3.6. Challenges with Geospatial Data#

Despite its numerous advantages, geospatial data also poses certain challenges:

  • Data Quality: Ensuring the accuracy and timeliness of geospatial data is crucial, as inaccuracies can have significant real-world implications.

  • Data Integration: Merging geospatial data from diverse sources and standards necessitates robust integration techniques.

  • Privacy Concerns: The collection and usage of geospatial data must respect the privacy rights of individuals.
