2.8. Spatial Frameworks#

Spatial frameworks form the foundation of Geographic Information Systems (GIS), providing the essential structure for organizing and analyzing geospatial data. These frameworks translate real-world locations into a format that can be managed and analyzed within GIS environments [Longley et al., 1999].

2.8.1. Key Components#

2.8.1.1. Spatial Reference Systems (SRS)#

SRS provide rules for mapping Earth’s surface points to coordinates, enabling integration of spatial data from various sources[3]. Examples include:

  • World Geodetic System 1984 (WGS 84), used by GPS

  • EPSG:3401, a projected coordinate system for Alberta, Canada

2.8.1.2. Geodetic Networks#

These networks consist of physical reference points on Earth’s surface, serving as the basis for mapping and surveying[1]. The National Spatial Reference System (NSRS) in the United States is an example, comprising over 300,000 geodetic points.

2.8.1.3. Grid Systems#

Virtual grids overlaid on Earth’s surface aid in referencing spatial data[3]. These can be global (e.g., latitude and longitude) or local (e.g., city planning grids). The Universal Transverse Mercator (UTM) is a widely used grid system that divides the world into 6-degree longitudinal zones.

2.8.1.4. Spatial Data Models#

These models define how spatial data is represented within GIS[3]. They include:

  • Vector-based models: Represent discrete objects like roads and buildings

  • Raster-based models: Represent continuous data like elevation or temperature

2.8.2. Evolution of Spatial Frameworks#

Modern GIS technology has expanded the capabilities of spatial frameworks, integrating desktop tools with web-based services for more dynamic and accessible spatial analysis[3]. This shift towards a web-centric paradigm requires spatial frameworks to be adaptable to both traditional desktop GIS environments and modern web-based platforms.

2.8.3. Importance in GIS Operations#

Spatial frameworks are essential in GIS operations for two main reasons[1]:

  1. They provide spatial context and structure, facilitating linkage to the real world and enabling operations like image rectification.

  2. They serve as a mechanism to register different datasets together in their correct relative positions, ensuring proper relationships between component objects within each dataset.

2.8.4. Challenges and Considerations#

While an ideal framework is desirable, resource constraints, historical legacies, changing user needs, and new technologies often necessitate trade-offs[1]. Additionally, the growing need for multi-national and globally consistent frameworks presents new challenges and opportunities for collaboration in framework development and maintenance[1].

2.9. Spatial Frameworks#

At the heart of any GIS is the spatial framework, which is essentially the scaffolding that supports all subsequent layers of geospatial data. It’s the foundation upon which we build our understanding of geographic spaces and their relationships. A spatial framework takes real-world locations and translates them into a format that can be easily managed and analyzed within a GIS.

Key Components of Spatial Frameworks:

  1. Spatial Reference Systems (SRS): These systems provide a set of rules for how points on the Earth’s surface are mapped to coordinates. They ensure that spatial data from different sources can be integrated and compared in a meaningful way.

    Example:: The World Geodetic System 1984 (WGS 84) is a global reference system for geospatial information and is the reference coordinate system used by the Global Positioning System (GPS).

    Example:: EPSG:3401 refers to a specific projected coordinate system used for spatial data management in Canada, particularly in Alberta. It’s part of the EPSG (European Petroleum Survey Group) Geodetic Parameter Dataset, which is a collection of definitions of coordinate reference systems and coordinate transformations.

    • Coordinate System: NAD83 / Alberta 10-TM (Resource)

    • Area of Use: Canada - Alberta

    • Details: This system uses the North American Datum of 1983 (NAD83) and applies a Transverse Mercator projection tailored for the 10th meridian west in Alberta. It’s designed for resource management and has negative easting coordinates in western Alberta, which means the coordinate values west of the central meridian are negative.

    For an alternative system with positive easting coordinates, the NAD83 / Alberta 10-TM (Forest) with the code “EPSG:3400” can be used. The choice between these systems depends on the specific requirements of the spatial data application in use.

  2. Geodetic Networks: These are networks of physical reference points on the Earth’s surface that provide accurate, measured locations that serve as the basis for mapping and surveying.

    Example:: The National Spatial Reference System (NSRS) in the United States is a network of over 300,000 geodetic points that are used for mapping and surveying across the United States.

  3. Grid Systems: Virtual grids overlaid on the Earth’s surface help in referencing spatial data. They can be global, like the lines of latitude and longitude, or local, like the grids used for city planning.

    Example:: The Universal Transverse Mercator (UTM) is a type of grid system that divides the world into a series of 6-degree longitudinal zones for detailed mapping and navigation.

  4. Spatial Data Models: These models define how spatial data is represented within the GIS. They can be vector-based, representing discrete objects like roads and buildings, or raster-based, representing continuous data like elevation or temperature.

    Example:: A vector data model might represent a road network in a city, while a raster data model could represent the varying elevations across a mountain range.

The evolution of GIS technology has expanded the capabilities of spatial frameworks. Modern GIS now integrates desktop tools with web-based services, allowing for more dynamic and accessible spatial analysis. This shift towards a web-centric paradigm means that spatial frameworks must be adaptable to both traditional desktop GIS environments and modern web-based platforms.
