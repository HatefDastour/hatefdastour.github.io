2.3. Map Projections#

Maps might seem simple, but they are quite complex, often containing multiple layers of information. We can create interactive maps by layering different datasets, but we must be cautious. Familiarity with maps doesn’t guarantee accuracy or understanding. Projections are a prime example.

The Earth isn’t a perfect sphere; it’s an oblate spheroid due to its chemical composition and the centrifugal force from its rotation, making it bulge at the equator. This shape is technically an ellipsoid, with a shorter circumference around the poles than the equator. To create a two-dimensional map, we use a geographic coordinate system with latitude and longitude lines, called a graticule, to project this imperfect sphere onto a flat surface. These projections simplify complex factors like changes in Earth’s gravitational field due to topography, known as the geoid. It’s crucial to recognize that the entire world can’t be accurately represented on a flat surface without some distortion [McClain, 2022].

Fig. 2.3 illustrates the concept of map projections by showing a globe with latitude and longitude lines, known as a graticule. Here’s a breakdown of the key elements:

  1. Latitude and Longitude Lines: These lines form a grid over the Earth’s surface. Latitude lines run horizontally and measure the distance north or south of the Equator (0° Latitude). Longitude lines run vertically and measure the distance east or west of the Prime Meridian (0° Longitude).

  2. Key Locations: The figure marks significant points such as the North Pole (90° N Latitude) and the Equator. It also labels continents like North America, Europe, Asia, and Africa.

  3. Ellipsoid Shape: The globe’s shape is shown as an ellipsoid, highlighting that the Earth’s circumference is shorter around the poles than around the equator.

  4. Projection Process: The figure demonstrates how the three-dimensional shape of the Earth is translated onto a flat surface using the graticule. This process involves projecting the curved surface of the Earth onto a two-dimensional plane, which inevitably leads to some distortion.

2.3.1. The Geoid#

The geoid is a complex model that represents the Earth’s true shape more accurately than a simple ellipsoid. It is an equipotential surface of the Earth’s gravity field that closely approximates mean sea level. In essence, the geoid represents the shape that the ocean surface would take under the influence of Earth’s gravitation and rotation alone, without other influences such as winds and tides [Freymueller and Sánchez, 2023, Torge et al., 2023].

Fig. 2.4 is a color-coded map that illustrates the undulation of the geoid in meters, based on the EGM96 gravity model and the WGS84 reference ellipsoid. The colors range from deep blue to red, indicating variations in geoid height from approximately -100 to +80 meters. This map uses a rectangular projection with latitude lines ranging from 75° North to 75° South and longitude lines from 180° West to 180° East. The variations in color represent the irregular shape of Earth’s gravity field, which deviates from a perfect ellipsoid due to uneven mass distribution within the planet.

2.3.1.1. Key Characteristics of the Geoid#

Key characteristics of the geoid include [Freymueller and Sánchez, 2023, Kumar et al., 2023]:

  1. Irregular shape: The geoid has an uneven, wavy surface due to the Earth’s uneven mass distribution and gravitational variations. Unlike the smooth mathematical ellipsoid, the geoid reflects the actual gravitational field of the Earth.

  2. Gravitational equipotential surface: The geoid represents the shape the ocean surface would take under the influence of Earth’s gravity and rotation, without other factors like winds and tides. All points on the geoid have the same gravitational potential.

  3. Reference for elevation: The geoid serves as a reference surface for measuring orthometric heights, which are commonly understood as heights above sea level.

  4. Relationship to mean sea level: The geoid closely approximates the mean sea level, extended through the continents.

  5. Geoid undulation: The difference between the geoid and the reference ellipsoid is called geoid undulation or geoidal height. This can vary by up to 100 meters in different locations.

  6. Gravity anomalies: The geoid’s shape reflects local variations in the Earth’s gravity field, with rises in areas of higher density and dips in areas of lower density.

2.3.1.2. Importance of the Geoid#

The geoid is important for several reasons [Seeber, 2008, Sjöberg and Bagherbandi, 2017]:

  1. Accurate elevation measurements: It provides a more precise reference for determining elevations than a simple ellipsoid model.

  2. GPS height conversion: The geoid is crucial for converting GPS-derived ellipsoidal heights to practical orthometric heights.

  3. Earth science applications: Understanding the geoid is essential for various fields, including oceanography, hydrology, and geology.

  4. Improved mapping and surveying: The geoid enables more accurate cartography, digital terrain models, and satellite altimetry.

  5. Gravity field studies: The geoid helps in studying the Earth’s gravity field and its variations over time.

Determining the precise shape of the geoid is an ongoing challenge in geodesy. Modern techniques combine satellite data (from missions like GRACE and GOCE) with terrestrial gravity measurements to create increasingly accurate geoid models. These improved models enhance our understanding of Earth’s shape, gravity field, and various geophysical processes.
