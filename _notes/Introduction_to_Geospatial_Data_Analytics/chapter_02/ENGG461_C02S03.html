2.3. Map Projections#

Maps might seem simple, but they are quite complex, often containing multiple layers of information. We can create interactive maps by layering different datasets, but we must be cautious. Familiarity with maps doesn’t guarantee accuracy or understanding. Projections are a prime example.

The Earth isn’t a perfect sphere; it’s an oblate spheroid due to its chemical composition and the centrifugal force from its rotation, making it bulge at the equator. This shape is technically an ellipsoid, with a shorter circumference around the poles than the equator. To create a two-dimensional map, we use a geographic coordinate system with latitude and longitude lines, called a graticule, to project this imperfect sphere onto a flat surface. These projections simplify complex factors like changes in Earth’s gravitational field due to topography, known as the geoid. It’s crucial to recognize that the entire world can’t be accurately represented on a flat surface without some distortion [McClain, 2022].

Fig. 2.2 illustrates the concept of map projections by showing a globe with latitude and longitude lines, known as a graticule. Here’s a breakdown of the key elements:

  1. Latitude and Longitude Lines: These lines form a grid over the Earth’s surface. Latitude lines run horizontally and measure the distance north or south of the Equator (0° Latitude). Longitude lines run vertically and measure the distance east or west of the Prime Meridian (0° Longitude).

  2. Key Locations: The figure marks significant points such as the North Pole (90° N Latitude) and the Equator. It also labels continents like North America, Europe, Asia, and Africa.

  3. Ellipsoid Shape: The globe’s shape is shown as an ellipsoid, highlighting that the Earth’s circumference is shorter around the poles than around the equator.

  4. Projection Process: The figure demonstrates how the three-dimensional shape of the Earth is translated onto a flat surface using the graticule. This process involves projecting the curved surface of the Earth onto a two-dimensional plane, which inevitably leads to some distortion.

2.3.1. The Geoid#

The geoid is a complex model that represents the Earth’s true shape more accurately than a simple ellipsoid. It is an equipotential surface of the Earth’s gravity field that closely approximates mean sea level. In essence, the geoid represents the shape that the ocean surface would take under the influence of Earth’s gravitation and rotation alone, without other influences such as winds and tides [Freymueller and Sánchez, 2023, Torge et al., 2023].

Fig. 2.3 is a color-coded map that illustrates the undulation of the geoid in meters, based on the EGM96 gravity model and the WGS84 reference ellipsoid. The colors range from deep blue to red, indicating variations in geoid height from approximately -100 to +80 meters. This map uses a rectangular projection with latitude lines ranging from 75° North to 75° South and longitude lines from 180° West to 180° East. The variations in color represent the irregular shape of Earth’s gravity field, which deviates from a perfect ellipsoid due to uneven mass distribution within the planet.

Key characteristics of the geoid include [Freymueller and Sánchez, 2023, Kumar et al., 2023]:

  1. Irregular shape: Unlike the smooth ellipsoid, the geoid has an irregular, lumpy surface due to variations in the Earth’s mass distribution and topography.

  2. Gravity-based: It is defined by Earth’s gravity field, making it useful for precise elevation measurements.

  3. Reference for elevation: The geoid serves as the zero surface for measuring orthometric heights (elevations above sea level).

The geoid is crucial in mapping and surveying for several reasons [Freymueller and Sánchez, 2023, Kumar et al., 2023]:

  1. It provides accurate vertical measurements in surveying and mapping.

  2. It helps in converting between ellipsoidal heights (from GPS) and orthometric heights (elevations above sea level).

  3. Understanding the geoid is essential for precise geodetic work, including the creation of accurate digital elevation models.

The relationship between the geoid and the ellipsoid is important to understand:

  • While the ellipsoid is a mathematical approximation of Earth’s shape, the geoid represents a more physically meaningful surface based on gravity.

  • The difference between the geoid and the reference ellipsoid is called the geoid undulation or geoid height.

Geodesists use various methods to determine the geoid’s shape, including gravity measurements, satellite observations, and computational models. The geoid’s surface can vary by up to 100 meters from the ellipsoid, with some areas rising above and others dipping below the ellipsoid surface.

In practice, the geoid is used to convert GPS-derived ellipsoidal heights to more practical orthometric heights (elevations above sea level). This conversion is crucial for many applications in surveying, construction, and earth sciences.

Understanding the geoid is essential for accurate mapping, navigation, and Earth science studies. It provides a more realistic representation of the Earth’s shape and gravity field, allowing for more precise measurements and calculations in various fields of study and practical applications
