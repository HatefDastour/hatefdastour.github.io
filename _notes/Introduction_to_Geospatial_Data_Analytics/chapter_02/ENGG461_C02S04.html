2.4. Types of Map Projections#

2.4.1. Cylindrical Projections#

Cylindrical projections wrap a cylinder around the globe. The Mercator projection is a well-known example that preserves angles, making it useful for navigation despite distorting areas, particularly near the poles. This type of projection is effective for equatorial regions but can misrepresent the size of landmasses at higher latitudes [Kumar et al., 2023, McHaffie et al., 2023].

Fig. 2.4 illustrates the central cylindrical projection method used in cartography:

  1. Semitransparent Globe: The figure shows a globe with a grid of meridians and parallels, representing Earth’s surface.

  2. Light Source: A light is placed at the center of the globe.

  3. Photographic Film: A cylindrical piece of photographic film is wrapped around the globe.

  4. Projection Process: The light source projects the grid from the globe onto the cylindrical film, resulting in a distorted representation of Earth’s surface on the flat film.

This method demonstrates how a three-dimensional object (the globe) can be projected onto a two-dimensional surface (the film), which is a fundamental concept in map-making. The central cylindrical projection is particularly useful for understanding how different map projections handle the challenge of representing the curved surface of the Earth on a flat plane.

For the central cylindrical projection, the coordinates \((x, y)\) on the map are derived from the latitude \(\phi\) and longitude \(\lambda\) as follows:

\[\begin{equation*} x = R (\lambda - \lambda_0) \end{equation*}\]
\[\begin{equation*} y = R \tan(\phi) \end{equation*}\]

Where:

  • \(x\) and \(y\) are the coordinates on the flat map

  • \(R\) is the radius of the globe

  • \(\lambda\) is the longitude

  • \(\lambda_0\) is the longitude of the central meridian

  • \(\phi\) is the latitude

2.4.1.1. Characteristics#

Characteristics of cylindrical projections [Hinks, 2016]:

  1. Not Conformal: This projection does not preserve angles, meaning that the shape of geographical features is distorted, especially as you move away from the equator.

  2. Not Equal-Area: Areas are not preserved in this projection. Regions far from the equator appear much larger than they actually are.

  3. Equator: The equator is represented as a straight line and is the only parallel (line of latitude) that’s true to scale.

  4. Meridians: All meridians (lines of longitude) are equally spaced vertical straight lines.

  5. Poles: The poles cannot be shown on this projection because as \(\phi\) approaches ±90°, \(\tan(\phi)\) approaches infinity.

2.4.1.2. Distortion#

Distortion increases rapidly as you move away from the equator. This is because the \(\tan(\phi)\) function grows very quickly as \(\phi\) approaches \(\pm\)90° [Hinks, 2016]:

  • Near the equator, the distortion is minimal.

  • At about 45° latitude, the scale is already doubled compared to the equator.

  • Beyond 60° latitude, the distortion becomes severe, making the projection impractical for representing high latitude areas.

Due to these characteristics, the central cylindrical projection is rarely used for world maps. It’s mostly of theoretical interest or used for specialized purposes where its unique properties are beneficial.

2.4.1.3. Conic Projections#

In conic projections, the Earth’s surface is projected onto a cone. These projections are particularly suited for mapping mid-latitude regions, where they maintain a balance between shape and area distortion. The Albers Equal Area and Lambert Conformal Conic projections are common examples, each serving specific purposes in thematic mapping [Kumar et al., 2023, McHaffie et al., 2023].

Fig. 2.5 shows a world map using the equidistant conic projection. This type of projection is characterized by:

  • 15° Graticule: The grid lines (graticule) are spaced at 15-degree intervals, helping to visualize the geographic coordinates.

  • Standard Parallels at 20°N and 60°N: These are the latitudes where the scale distortion is minimized. The map is most accurate along these lines, making it particularly useful for regions in the mid-latitudes.

  • Representation: The map accurately represents distances along meridians, making it useful for regional maps that require equal distances along these lines.

This projection is often used for thematic and regional maps because it balances the representation of area, shape, and distance, especially in mid-latitude regions.

2.4.1.4. Azimuthal Projections#

Azimuthal projections project the Earth’s surface onto a plane, typically touching the globe at a single point. These projections are often used for polar regions and can preserve either distances or directions from the center point. The Stereographic projection is a notable example, allowing for accurate representation of circular areas around the point of contact [Kumar et al., 2023, McHaffie et al., 2023].

Fig. 2.6 shows a map of the Earth using the azimuthal equidistant projection centered on the North Pole. Key features include:

  • Central Point: The North Pole is the center of the map.

  • Distance and Direction: Distances from the North Pole to any other point on the map are proportionally correct, and directions from the North Pole are accurately represented.

  • Coverage: The map extends all the way to the South Pole, showing all continents and oceans.

  • Distortion: Distortion increases as you move away from the center, especially near the edges.

This projection is useful for applications like radio and seismic mapping, where accurate distances from a central point are crucial.

2.4.2. Choosing the Right Projection#

The choice of a map projection depends on the purpose of the map:

  • Preserving Area: Equal-area projections, like the Albers Equal Area, maintain the relative size of areas across the map.

  • Preserving Shape: Conformal projections, like the Lambert Conformal Conic, maintain the shape of small areas.

  • Preserving Distance: Equidistant projections maintain accurate distances from certain points or along certain lines.

  • Preserving Direction: Azimuthal projections can maintain accurate directions from the central point to any other point on the map.

2.4.3. The Impact of Projections#

The selection of a map projection can influence how we perceive spatial relationships and can have implications for everything from navigation to political perceptions. For example, the size distortion of the Mercator projection has been criticized for giving an inflated sense of the size of higher-latitude countries.
