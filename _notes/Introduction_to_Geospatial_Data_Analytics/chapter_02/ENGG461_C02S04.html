2.4. Types of Map Projections#

Map projections are methods of representing the curved surface of the Earth on a flat map. Each type of projection has its own characteristics, advantages, and limitations. The main types of map projections include:

  1. Cylindrical Projections:

    • These projections are created by wrapping a cylinder around the globe. They are useful for equatorial regions but can distort areas at higher latitudes.

    • Examples:: Mercator, Plate Carree, and Central Cylindrical.

  2. Conic Projections:

    • These involve projecting the Earth’s surface onto a cone. They are well-suited for mid-latitude regions and balance shape and area distortion.

    • Examples:: Albers Equal Area and Lambert Conformal Conic.

  3. Azimuthal (or Planar) Projections:

    • These projections map the Earth onto a plane. They are often used for polar regions and are useful for displaying great circle routes.

    • Examples:: Stereographic, Orthographic, and Azimuthal Equidistant.

2.4.1. Cylindrical Projections#

Cylindrical projections wrap a cylinder around the globe. The Mercator projection is a well-known example that preserves angles, making it useful for navigation despite distorting areas, particularly near the poles. This type of projection is effective for equatorial regions but can misrepresent the size of landmasses at higher latitudes [Kumar et al., 2023, McHaffie et al., 2023].

Fig. 2.5 illustrates the central cylindrical projection method used in cartography:

  1. Semitransparent Globe: The figure shows a globe with a grid of meridians and parallels, representing Earth’s surface.

  2. Light Source: A light is placed at the center of the globe.

  3. Photographic Film: A cylindrical piece of photographic film is wrapped around the globe.

  4. Projection Process: The light source projects the grid from the globe onto the cylindrical film, resulting in a distorted representation of Earth’s surface on the flat film.

This method demonstrates how a three-dimensional object (the globe) can be projected onto a two-dimensional surface (the film), which is a fundamental concept in map-making. The central cylindrical projection is particularly useful for understanding how different map projections handle the challenge of representing the curved surface of the Earth on a flat plane.

For the central cylindrical projection, the coordinates \((x, y)\) on the map are derived from the latitude \(\phi\) and longitude \(\lambda\) as follows:

(2.1)#\[\begin{split}\begin{cases} x = R (\lambda - \lambda_0), \\ ~\\ y = R \tan(\phi). \end{cases} \end{split}\]

Where:

  • \(x\) and \(y\) are the coordinates on the flat map

  • \(R\) is the radius of the globe

  • \(\lambda\) is the longitude

  • \(\lambda_0\) is the longitude of the central meridian

  • \(\phi\) is the latitude

Example 2.7

Calgary is located at approximately 51.0447° N latitude and 114.0719° W longitude. We will use these coordinates in the formulas provided (equation (2.1)):

  1. Convert Degrees to Radians:

    \[\begin{equation*}\lambda = -114.0719^\circ \times \dfrac{\pi}{180} = -1.9909 \, \text{radians}\end{equation*}\]
    \[\begin{equation*}\phi = 51.0447^\circ \times \dfrac{\pi}{180} = 0.8909 \, \text{radians}\end{equation*}\]

  2. Calculate x-coordinate:

    \[\begin{align*}x &= R (\lambda - \lambda_0) = 6,371 \, \text{km} \times (-1.9909 \, \text{radians} - 0 \, \text{radians}) \\&= -12684.22 \, \text{km}\end{align*}\]

  3. Calculate y-coordinate:

    \[\begin{align*}y &= R \tan(\phi) = 6,371 \, \text{km} \times \tan(0.8909 \, \text{radians}) \\&= 6,371 \, \text{km} \times 1.236869 = 7880.09 \, \text{km}\end{align*}\]

The left plot uses an orthographic projection to offer a 3D-like view of the Earth, centered on Calgary, highlighting its global position. The orthographic projection is not a cylindrical projection but rather a perspective projection that resembles how the Earth would appear from space.

Note

The 2D map in Example 2.7 uses the Plate Carree projection, also known as the equirectangular projection, is a simple and practical cylindrical projection. The Plate Carree projection is characterized by its straightforward mapping of latitude and longitude directly to x and y coordinates on a plane, making it a simple and widely used projection for global maps.

Plate Carree Projection:

  • Type: Cylindrical projection.

  • Characteristics: Each degree of latitude and longitude corresponds to a uniform distance on the map. This projection is not conformal, meaning it does not preserve angles, and it is not equal-area, meaning it does not preserve area proportions.

  • Use Case: It is often used for simple geographic maps where preserving angles or areas is not crucial. It’s particularly useful for visualizing data that is already in a latitude-longitude format.

2.4.2. Characteristics of Cylindrical Projections#

Cylindrical projections have several key characteristics [Hinks, 2016]:

  1. Non-Conformal: These projections do not preserve angles, resulting in shape distortion, especially as you move away from the equator.

  2. Non-Equal-Area: Areas are not accurately represented; regions far from the equator appear larger than they are.

  3. Equator Representation: The equator is depicted as a straight line and is the only parallel that maintains true scale.

  4. Meridian Representation: Meridians are equally spaced vertical lines.

  5. Pole Representation: The poles cannot be shown because as latitude (\(\phi\)) approaches ±90°, \(\tan(\phi)\) approaches infinity.

2.4.3. Distortion in Cylindrical Projections#

Distortion in cylindrical projections increases rapidly with distance from the equator due to the behavior of the \(\tan(\phi)\) function [Hinks, 2016]:

  • Minimal Distortion Near the Equator: Distortion is least at the equator.

  • Increased Distortion at Mid-Latitudes: At approximately 45° latitude, the scale is doubled compared to the equator.

  • Severe Distortion at High Latitudes: Beyond 60° latitude, distortion becomes severe, making these projections impractical for high-latitude regions.

Due to these characteristics, the central cylindrical projection is seldom used for world maps and is mainly of theoretical interest or for specialized applications where its unique properties are advantageous.
