Polynomial and the Degree of the Polynomial

An expression of the form \(a_nx^n+a_{n-1}x^{n-1}+a_{n-2}x^{n-2}+\ldots+a_{1}x^{1}+a_0\) is called a polynomial. In this expression \(a_{0},~a_{1},~\ldots,~a_{n}\) are numbers and \(x\) is a variable. If the coeficient of \(x^n\) is nonzero (\(a_n \neq 0\)), the integer \(n\) is called the degree of the polynomial, and \(a_n\) is called the leading coefficient.

A polynomial of degree \(1\)

\(ax+b\)

A polynomial of degree \(2\)

\(ax^2+bx+c\)

A polynomial of degree \(3\)

\(ax^3+bx^2+cx+d\)

\(\vdots\)

\(\vdots\)

A polynomial of degree \(n\)

\(a_nx^n+a_{n-1}x^{n-1}+a_{n-2}x^{n-2}+\ldots+a_{1}x^{1}+a_0\)

Weierstrass Approximation Theorem

Let \(f\in C[a,b]\). Then for each \(\varepsilon>0\), there is a polynomial \(P(x)\) such that

\[\begin{align*} |f (x) − P(x)| < \varepsilon,\quad x\in[a,b]. \end{align*}\]
Interpolating Polynomial

Assume that \(n\) data pairs \((x_1,y_1 )\), \((x_2 , y_2 )\), …, \((x_n , y_n )\) are available in a way that the \(x_i\) are distinct. Then, there exists a unique polynomial

\[\begin{equation*} p(x) = r_0 +r_1 x+r_2 x^2 +\ldots+r_{n-1} x^{n-1} \end{equation*}\]

such that \(p(x_i ) = y_i\) for each \(i = 1, 2, \ldots, n\).

For this data, this polynomial is called the interpolating polynomial.
