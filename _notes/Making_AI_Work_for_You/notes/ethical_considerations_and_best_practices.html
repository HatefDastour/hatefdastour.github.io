6. Ethical Considerations and Best Practices#

Core Principle

Responsible AI use requires transparency, verification, privacy protection, and human oversight.

6.1. Verification: Your Responsibility#

You remain the author of record for all AI-assisted work. Suppose the AI makes errors—whether hallucinated citations, incorrect facts, or biased reasoning—you bear full responsibility. Always treat AI outputs as drafts and verify them yourself.

LLMs frequently generate plausible-looking citations that don’t exist. The verification workflow is simple: copy every citation into Google Scholar, PubMed, or your library database. Confirm the author, year, title, and journal match exactly. If not found, delete it immediately—it’s hallucinated [Ji et al., 2023].

Many models also have knowledge cutoffs, missing recent events or publications. For current information, use source-citing tools like Elicit, Consensus, or Scite that link directly to verifiable papers.

For critical conclusions, employ the “re-ask strategy”: pose the same question differently, from multiple angles. If answers contradict, external verification becomes mandatory. All AI outputs should be considered starting points requiring human review.

6.2. Privacy and Data Security#

Commercial LLMs present serious FERPA risks. Never upload student names, IDs, grades, identifiable assignments, class rosters, or evaluation data. Safe alternatives include anonymized examples, aggregated data, and generic scenarios.

For research, remove all participant identifiers before upload. Consider local models like Ollama for sensitive data, check IRB protocols for AI tool use, and prioritize institutional LLM services with data protection agreements.

6.3. Disclosure and Transparency#

Disclose AI use when it substantially shapes content, structure, or ideas, particularly if outputs are used with minimal modification or when journals/disciplines require it. Grammar checking, format conversion, brainstorming (unused ideas), and verified literature searches typically require no disclosure.

Major citation styles now provide AI guidance: see the APA Style Blog on ChatGPT and MLA’s Generative AI resources. Note that while literature searching needs no disclosure, journals like Nature require Methods sections to acknowledge AI summarization tools.

Sample course policy:

AI Policy: Use permitted for brainstorming/outlining. All submitted work must be yours. You must understand and explain everything you submit, disclose AI use (“Used [tool] for [purpose]”), and never submit AI-generated text as your own without substantial original development.

6.4. Bias and Representation#

LLMs exhibit predictable biases: English/US-centric perspectives, Western/male defaults, STEM over-representation, and an “echo chamber” effect where they reflect and amplify biases in your prompts. AI writing detectors compound the problem, often flagging non-native English speakers unfairly [Deen, 2024, Doyen and Todirascu, 2025, Guo et al., 2024, Mittelstädt et al., 2024, Sharma et al., 2024].

Counter these risks through inclusive prompting. Instead of “typical manager case study,” request “three managers from different industries, genders, and cultural backgrounds.” Always check outputs for generic examples, stereotypes, missing perspectives, and unstated cultural assumptions [Deen, 2024, Doyen and Todirascu, 2025, Guo et al., 2024, Mittelstädt et al., 2024, Sharma et al., 2024].

6.5. Academic Integrity#

For students, acceptable AI uses include brainstorming research questions (followed by independent development), draft feedback (with your review), concept explanations (rewritten in your words), and practice problem generation. Never submit AI text as your own, use AI on take-home exams unless permitted, generate entire papers, or outsource learning itself.

Faculty and researchers may draft outlines, revise for clarity, generate code (thoroughly tested), and organize literature notes. Disclose substantial AI contributions per journal requirements, but never list AI as a co-author, claim unverified outputs as original research, or fabricate data/references.

The key test remains: can you confidently defend this work without the AI?

6.6. Environmental Impact#

High-performance “frontier” models (like GPT-4 and upcoming iterations like GPT-5) require massive compute. A single complex query can consume as much energy as charging a smartphone by 1% to 5%. To practice “Sustainable AI,” use smaller, specialized models (e.g., Llama-3-8B or Mistral) for simple tasks and reserve frontier models for complex reasoning [Luccioni et al., 2022].

6.7. Best Practices Summary#

  • Check ALL citations in Scholar/PubMed

  • Re-ask critical questions from different angles

  • Cross-reference facts with primary sources

  • Test all generated code

  • External verification for contradictions

  • De-identify all data before upload

  • Local models for sensitive research

  • FERPA/IRB compliance mandatory

  • Institutional LLMs only for student data

  • Cite AI per APA/MLA guidelines

  • Methods disclosure for AI summaries (Nature policy)

  • Transparent modeling for students

  • Request diverse examples explicitly

  • Check for echo chamber reinforcement

  • Verify demographic representation

Guiding Questions

Before any AI task, ask:

  1. Does this data need privacy protection?

  2. How will I verify accuracy?

  3. Should I document this AI use?

  4. Are diverse perspectives represented?

  5. Am I avoiding necessary cognitive work?
