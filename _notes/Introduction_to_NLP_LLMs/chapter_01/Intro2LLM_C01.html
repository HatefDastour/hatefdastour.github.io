1. Introduction to Natural Language Processing and Large Language Models#

Natural Language Processing (NLP) sits at the intersection of computer science, linguistics, and statistics, focusing on how we can teach machines to work with human language at scale. From search engines and virtual assistants to machine translation and scientific text mining, modern NLP systems now shape how we access knowledge, communicate, and make decisions.

In recent years, large language models (LLMs) such as GPT‑style models have dramatically changed the way we design and deploy NLP systems. Instead of building bespoke pipelines for each task, we can increasingly treat an LLM as a powerful, adaptable component that can be steered through prompting and light-weight customization.

This chapter lays the foundation for that modern view of NLP and LLMs. We start with the basics of what it means to treat text as data, how we acquire and curate textual corpora, and which preprocessing steps are necessary before any modeling. We then connect these practical steps to linguistic structure and the historical evolution of NLP, and close with an overview of contemporary modeling and evaluation strategies that will be developed in later chapters.

In this chapter, you will learn to:

  • Explain what NLP is and articulate where LLMs fit into the broader NLP ecosystem.

  • Identify common NLP tasks (e.g., classification, tagging, sequence labeling, question answering) and how they map to real applications.

  • Acquire textual data responsibly via web scraping, public APIs, document processing, and open repositories.

  • Apply core text preprocessing and normalization steps, including cleaning, tokenization, sentence segmentation, stopword removal, stemming, and lemmatization.

  • Recognize key linguistic concepts (morphology, part-of-speech tags, syntax) and why they still matter in an LLM-dominated landscape.

  • Describe the high-level architecture and capabilities of modern language models and understand basic evaluation ideas.

The chapter is organized into five main sections, each corresponding to a teaching session and accompanied by Jupyter-based labs and practice notebooks:

  1. What is NLP and where do LLMs fit?
    We introduce the core goals of NLP, the notion of a corpus, and the standard NLP pipeline. You will see how tasks such as sentiment analysis, named entity recognition, and summarization can be approached with both classical and LLM-based methods.

  2. Foundations and data acquisition for NLP.
    We discuss where text data comes from in practice—web pages, APIs, document repositories, and scanned material—and how to collect it responsibly. This section is tightly connected to labs on web scraping and OCR, where you implement practical pipelines for building your own corpora.

  3. Text preprocessing and normalization.
    Raw text is messy: it contains HTML markup, emojis, punctuation, casing variation, and domain-specific noise. We cover the main techniques to clean and normalize text, including tokenization, sentence segmentation, stopword removal, stemming, and lemmatization. The labs provide hands-on experience implementing these steps with Python.

  4. Advanced linguistic analysis and the evolution of NLP.
    We move from surface-level preprocessing to richer linguistic structure. You will see how part-of-speech tagging, syntactic parsing, and simple semantic representations were central to earlier generations of NLP systems, and how these ideas persist beneath modern neural and transformer-based models.

  5. Modern modeling and evaluation strategies.
    Finally, we place large language models into context. We compare classical feature-based models, word embeddings, and neural architectures, introduce transformer-based LLMs at a high level, and discuss evaluation: intrinsic vs. extrinsic metrics, human evaluations, and emerging concerns around robustness, bias, and safety.

Throughout the chapter, code examples are drawn from the accompanying labs and practice notebooks. Each section includes short, focused Python snippets that mirror the workflows you will implement more fully in the Jupyter notebooks—such as scraping text with requests and BeautifulSoup, normalizing text with regex and nltk/spaCy, and experimenting with simple NLP models.
