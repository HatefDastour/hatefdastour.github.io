1.3. Text Preprocessing and Normalization#

1.3.1. Learning objectives#

By the end of this section, you should be able to:

  • Explain why text preprocessing and normalization are needed in NLP workflows.

  • Implement common preprocessing operations in Python (cleaning, tokenization, sentence segmentation).

  • Distinguish between stemming and lemmatization and understand when each is appropriate.

  • Discuss situations where aggressive normalization is harmful, especially with LLM prompts.

  • Describe the role of subword tokenization in modern language models.

1.3.2. Why preprocessing?#

Raw text is messy. Before we can train models or call an LLM API, we almost always need to clean and normalize our input. Reasons include:

  • Removing artifacts of data collection (HTML tags, boilerplate, markup).

  • Standardizing variations that do not carry meaning for the task (case, punctuation, spacing).

  • Preparing data for specific models or toolkits (e.g., tokenization requirements).

At the same time, over-preprocessing can destroy signal. A key theme of this section is learning how to balance simplicity and preservation of meaning.

In applied settings—such as extracting information from environmental impact reports or triaging public-health bulletins—the preprocessing decisions you make can materially change the conclusions you draw from the data. It is therefore important to treat preprocessing as part of the modeling design, not just a mechanical first step.

1.3.3. Cleaning and normalization#

The goal of cleaning and normalization is to reduce superficial variation that does not carry meaning for our task. Typical operations include:

  • Lowercasing.

  • Removing or standardizing punctuation.

  • Handling digits, URLs, emojis, and hashtags.

  • Collapsing repeated whitespace.

import re

def basic_clean(text: str) -> str:
    text = text.lower()
    text = re.sub(r"https?://\S+", " URL ", text)   # replace URLs
    text = re.sub(r"\s+", " ", text)               # collapse whitespace
    text = re.sub(r"[^a-z0-9\s]", " ", text)       # keep letters, digits, spaces
    return text.strip()

raw = "NLP in 2026: Emojis 🤖, URLs https://example.com, and #hashtags!"
print(basic_clean(raw))

nlp in 2026  emojis    urls     and  hashtags

In practice, the exact normalization strategy should depend on your application. For example, hashtags and emojis may carry important sentiment or topic information; in those cases, you might map them to special tokens instead of removing them.

1.3.3.1. Domain-aware normalization#

Different domains require different choices:

  • Scientific text: you may want to preserve equations, citations, and special notation.

  • Clinical notes: abbreviations and shorthand might be crucial; aggressive normalization can erase important cues.

  • Environmental and policy reports: numeric values, units, and geographic names are often critical and should be preserved carefully.

  • Social media: emojis, hashtags, and creative spellings often encode sentiment and stance.

When designing preprocessing pipelines, always ask: What information might my model need to solve the task?

1.3.4. Tokenization and sentence segmentation#

Tokenization splits text into meaningful units (tokens), such as words, subwords, or characters. Sentence segmentation splits a document into sentences. While simple str.split() tokenization can work for quick experiments, robust tokenization is essential for production systems.

import nltk

nltk.download("punkt", quiet=True)
nltk.download("punkt_tab", quiet=True)

text = "Transformers changed NLP. LLMs build on transformers."
sentences = nltk.sent_tokenize(text)
tokens = [nltk.word_tokenize(s) for s in sentences]

print("Sentences:", sentences)
print("Tokens:", tokens)

Sentences: ['Transformers changed NLP.', 'LLMs build on transformers.']
Tokens: [['Transformers', 'changed', 'NLP', '.'], ['LLMs', 'build', 'on', 'transformers', '.']]

Limitations of naive tokenization include:

  • Poor handling of contractions (don't, I'm).

  • Difficulty with languages that lack whitespace between words (e.g., Chinese, Japanese).

  • Challenges with multi-word expressions (e.g., “New York City”) and code-mixed text.

We will later see subword tokenization methods that better align with modern language models.

1.3.5. Stopwords, stemming, and lemmatization#

Many NLP tasks benefit from reducing inflected forms and removing very common function words. This section connects directly to the tokenization and normalization lab, where you experiment with stemming and lemmatization.

from nltk.corpus import stopwords
from nltk.stem import PorterStemmer, WordNetLemmatizer
import nltk

nltk.download("stopwords", quiet=True)
nltk.download("wordnet", quiet=True)

stops = set(stopwords.words("english"))
stemmer = PorterStemmer()
lemmatizer = WordNetLemmatizer()

tokens = ["running", "runs", "ran", "runner", "the", "and"]

filtered = [t for t in tokens if t.lower() not in stops]
stems = [stemmer.stem(t) for t in filtered]
lemmas = [lemmatizer.lemmatize(t, pos="v") for t in filtered]

print("Filtered:", filtered)
print("Stems   :", stems)
print("Lemmas  :", lemmas)

Filtered: ['running', 'runs', 'ran', 'runner']
Stems   : ['run', 'run', 'ran', 'runner']
Lemmas  : ['run', 'run', 'run', 'runner']

  • Stemming applies simple heuristic rules to strip affixes (e.g., running → run, runner → runner). It is fast but can produce non-words.

  • Lemmatization uses vocabulary and part-of-speech information to map words to their dictionary base forms (running → run, ran → run). It is usually more interpretable but may be slower and require additional resources.

Whether you should remove stopwords depends on the task. Removing them is common for topic modeling and document retrieval; keeping them is often better for tasks that depend on function words (e.g., question answering, entailment).

1.3.6. Subword tokenization and LLMs#

Modern LLMs rarely operate on whitespace-delimited words. Instead, they use subword tokenization schemes such as Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) or WordPiece:

  • Rare or complex words are broken into smaller units (e.g., internationalization → international@@ ization).

  • This mitigates out-of-vocabulary issues and allows compact vocabularies.

  • Tokenization becomes model-specific: the same string may be segmented differently by different models.

From a practical standpoint:

  • When using APIs or libraries for LLMs, you typically do not need to tokenize manually; the library handles it.

  • When training or fine-tuning models, you must use the same tokenizer as the base model.

Understanding that LLMs “see” subword tokens—not raw characters or words—is helpful when reasoning about context length limits, prompt engineering, and token-based pricing.

1.3.7. When normalization can hurt#

Although normalization is powerful, overly aggressive cleaning can remove important signal:

  • Stripping punctuation may erase cues such as question marks or exclamation points.

  • Lowercasing and removing diacritics may obscure proper names or language distinctions.

  • Removing stopwords may harm tasks that depend on function words (e.g., question answering, logical inference).

  • Replacing all digits with a generic token might hurt numerical reasoning tasks.

Modern LLMs are often robust to raw, noisy text because their tokenizers and training corpora cover many variants. However, thoughtful preprocessing remains important when you:

  • Train your own models on limited data.

  • Work with specialized domains (clinical notes, legal documents, scientific articles, environmental reports).

  • Need interpretable features and transparent transformations.

  • Want to reduce input length while preserving task-relevant information.

1.3.8. Exercises and discussion#

  1. Normalization design
    Pick a target task (e.g., sentiment analysis on movie reviews, topic modeling of news, QA over policy documents). Propose a preprocessing pipeline for that task. For each step, justify why it helps and what you might lose.

  2. Stopwords experiment (conceptual)
    Suppose you train two sentiment classifiers: one with stopwords removed and one with stopwords retained. In which cases might the stopword-free model perform worse? Give concrete examples.

  3. Prompting and preprocessing
    Consider an LLM-based summarization system. Would you aggressively normalize the input documents (lowercase, remove punctuation, remove stopwords) before sending them to the model? Why or why not? How might light normalization still be useful?
