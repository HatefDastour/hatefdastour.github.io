1.4. Advanced Linguistic Analysis and the Evolution of NLP#

1.4.1. Learning objectives#

By the end of this section, you should be able to:

  • Explain the role of morphology and part-of-speech (POS) tagging in NLP.

  • Describe the difference between syntactic structure (dependency vs. constituency) and why it matters.

  • Summarize classical semantic representations (bag-of-words, tf–idf, embeddings).

  • Place large language models within the historical evolution of NLP methods.

  • Interpret basic output from a modern linguistic analysis toolkit (spaCy).

1.4.2. Morphology and part-of-speech tagging#

Morphology studies how words are formed from smaller units (morphemes), such as roots and affixes:

  • Inflectional morphology: changes grammatical features (tense, number, gender) without altering core meaning: run → runs → running.

  • Derivational morphology: creates new words or changes word class: govern → government → governmental.

In NLP, we often approximate morphological information through part-of-speech (POS) tags, which label each token with a coarse grammatical category (noun, verb, adjective, etc.).

POS tagging serves several purposes:

  • Provides a compact summary of sentence structure.

  • Supports downstream tasks such as parsing, information extraction, and named entity recognition.

  • Improves feature representations for classical models (e.g., “verb lemmas in questions”).

Modern libraries such as spaCy make it easy to obtain POS tags and other annotations:

import spacy

nlp = spacy.load("en_core_web_sm")
doc = nlp("The large language model summarizes scientific articles accurately.")

for token in doc:
    print(f"{token.text:12s} {token.pos_:6s} {token.dep_:10s} head={token.head.text}")

The          DET    det        head=model
large        ADJ    amod       head=model
language     NOUN   compound   head=model
model        NOUN   nsubj      head=summarizes
summarizes   VERB   ROOT       head=summarizes
scientific   ADJ    amod       head=articles
articles     NOUN   dobj       head=summarizes
accurately   ADV    advmod     head=summarizes
.            PUNCT  punct      head=summarizes

This small example illustrates how each token is associated with:

  • A POS tag (token.pos_), such as NOUN, VERB, ADJ.

  • A dependency relation (token.dep_) that indicates its syntactic role (e.g., subject, object, modifier) relative to a head word.

In environmental and public-health contexts, POS and morphology can help us distinguish, for example, between mentions of diseases, interventions, and geographic locations, which often behave differently in sentences.

1.4.3. Syntactic structure#

Syntax concerns how words combine into phrases and sentences. Early NLP systems relied heavily on explicit syntactic representations such as:

  • Constituency trees: hierarchical structures grouping words into phrases (NP, VP, PP, etc.).

  • Dependency graphs: directed graphs where edges indicate head–dependent relations between words.

These structures support:

  • Question answering and information extraction (e.g., identifying who did what to whom).

  • Coreference resolution and discourse analysis.

  • Error analysis and interpretability of model behavior.

While large language models often learn to exploit syntactic regularities implicitly, explicit parses remain useful for many applications and research questions. They provide a level of abstraction that can be inspected and reasoned about directly.

1.4.3.1. Dependency vs. constituency (intuition)#

  • Constituency focuses on phrase structure: grouping tokens into nested constituents (e.g., [The large language model] [summarizes [scientific articles] [accurately]]).

  • Dependency focuses on head–modifier relationships: “summarizes” is the head; “model” is its subject; “articles” is its object; “accurately” is an adverbial modifier.

Most modern toolkits default to dependency parsing because it is compact and easy to align with downstream tasks.

1.4.4. Semantic representations#

Beyond syntax, we care about meaning. Classical NLP used several representation schemes:

  • Bag-of-words and tf–idf
    Represent documents as high-dimensional sparse vectors based on word counts, sometimes weighted to emphasize informative terms.

  • Distributed word embeddings (e.g., word2vec, GloVe, fastText)
    Map words to dense vectors such that semantically similar words are close in vector space (e.g., king – man + woman ≈ queen).

  • Sentence and document embeddings
    Aggregate word embeddings or learn dedicated encoders to obtain fixed-length representations of larger text units.

Modern transformer-based models learn contextual embeddings in which the representation of a word depends on its surrounding context. This addresses many limitations of static embeddings (e.g., polysemy) and provides a powerful foundation for downstream tasks.

In applied projects, you might compare tf–idf features to contextual embeddings when building systems that, for instance, retrieve similar environmental reports or cluster public-comments by theme.

1.4.5. Historical milestones in NLP#

It is useful to situate large language models within the broader history of NLP:

Table 1.2 Very high-level timeline of NLP methods#

Era

Dominant approach

Representative methods

Rule-based (1950s–1980s)

Hand-written grammars and rules

ELIZA, early MT systems

Statistical (1990s–2000s)

Probabilistic models over text and speech

N-grams, HMMs, CRFs, IBM MT models

Neural (2010–2016)

Learned features from embeddings and sequences

word2vec, RNNs, CNNs, seq2seq

Attention & Transformers (2016–2019)

Parallel sequence modeling

Transformer, BERT, GPT-2

Large Language Models (2019– )

Massive pretrained models with in-context learning

GPT-3+, PaLM, LLaMA, etc.

Key transitions:

  • From rules to statistics: data-driven models replacing hand-crafted grammars.

  • From statistics to neural: learned dense representations reducing the need for manual feature engineering.

  • From RNNs to transformers: attention mechanisms enabling efficient training on long sequences.

  • From transformers to LLMs: scaling data, parameters, and compute to achieve zero/few-shot capabilities across tasks.

Throughout this evolution, linguistic concepts like morphology, syntax, and semantics have remained relevant. Even when models do not explicitly expose these structures, they often benefit from training data and evaluation protocols that reflect them.

1.4.6. Linguistic structure and LLMs#

Although LLMs are not built as explicit linguistic pipelines, evidence suggests that:

  • They encode substantial information about POS, syntax, and semantics in their internal representations.

  • They can often be probed for linguistic knowledge (e.g., predicting grammaticality or agreement).

  • They may still struggle with phenomena requiring deep reasoning, world knowledge, or discourse tracking.

In later chapters, we will revisit these ideas when we analyze how transformer-based models encode linguistic structure and how we can probe and interpret their internal representations.

1.4.7. Exercises and discussion#

  1. POS tagging intuition
    Take a short paragraph (3–4 sentences) from a news article. Without any tools, manually annotate POS tags for each word. Then run the paragraph through spaCy and compare results. Where do you disagree? Why?

  2. Dependency vs. constituency
    Sketch both a constituency tree and a dependency tree for the sentence:
    “The rapidly changing climate is affecting coastal communities worldwide.”
    Discuss what each view makes easy or hard to see.

  3. Historical reflection
    Choose one era from the timeline (e.g., “statistical NLP”). What were its strengths and limitations compared to LLM-based methods? In what situations might a classical method still be preferable today?
