1.2. Data Acquisition for NLP#

1.2.1. Learning objectives#

By the end of this section, you should be able to:

  • Describe common sources of textual data for NLP (web, APIs, document repositories, logs).

  • Implement basic web scraping workflows with Python using requests and BeautifulSoup.

  • Explain where OCR fits into an NLP pipeline and understand its limitations.

  • Identify open repositories that provide NLP-ready text and understand how to search them.

  • Articulate ethical, legal, and documentation considerations when collecting text data.

1.2.2. Why data acquisition matters#

Modern NLP systems are only as good as the text data they are trained and evaluated on. High-quality, representative, and well-documented corpora are essential for:

  • Building robust models that generalize beyond narrow benchmarks.

  • Supporting domain-specific applications (e.g., clinical NLP, environmental policy analysis).

  • Enabling reproducible research and fair comparisons between systems.

In this section we survey practical ways to acquire textual data for your projects, from open government and research repositories to web scraping and document processing. Along the way, you will connect these ideas to hands-on labs on web scraping, OCR, and preprocessing.

1.2.3. Sources of textual data#

At a high level, most NLP corpora originate from one or more of the following sources:

  • Web content: news sites, blogs, forums, documentation, wikis.

  • APIs and data services: social media APIs, scholarly data APIs, open-data APIs.

  • Document repositories: PDFs, Word documents, reports, and books from institutional repositories.

  • Logs and conversational platforms: chat transcripts, customer-support conversations, issue trackers.

  • Specialized NLP repositories: pre-packaged datasets such as GLUE, SQuAD, or domain-specific corpora.

The challenge is not only to obtain text, but to do so responsibly and in a way that preserves relevant structure (e.g., headings, metadata, document boundaries).

In the context of environmental and public health applications, for example, relevant text sources include government climate assessments, flood and drought reports, policy briefs, and scientific literature. Building corpora from these sources allows us to analyze narratives around risk, adaptation, and policy at scale.

1.2.4. Web scraping: turning HTML into text#

Web pages are one of the most common sources of text for NLP. However, the text we care about is usually embedded inside HTML structure (tags, attributes, navigation links, ads). Our goal is to extract the content we need while respecting the website’s rules and avoiding unnecessary noise.

1.2.4.1. Example: scraping article titles with BeautifulSoup#

The following example mirrors the introductory web scraping labs and practices from this chapter. It uses requests to download a page and BeautifulSoup to parse the HTML and extract titles.

import requests
from bs4 import BeautifulSoup

url = "https://news.ycombinator.com/"
response = requests.get(url, timeout=10)
response.raise_for_status()

soup = BeautifulSoup(response.text, "html.parser")

titles = [
    a.get_text(strip=True)
    for a in soup.select("a.storylink, span.titleline > a")
]

for i, title in enumerate(titles[:10], start=1):
    print(f"{i:2d}. {title}")

 1. US healthcare marketplaces shared citizenship and race data with ad tech giants
 2. U.S. military data left exposed at an a16z startup for 150 days
 3. Removable batteries in smartphones will be mandatory in the EU starting in 2027
 4. I am worried about Bun
 5. Stop big tech from making users behave in ways they don't want to
 6. Days Without GitHub Incidents
 7. Redis array: short story of a long development process
 8. GitHub Is Down
 9. Talking to 35 Strangers at the Gym
10. I tracked 7,700 UK petrol stations every 10 minutes for 3 months

In your labs, you will adapt this pattern to scrape other sites (e.g., GitHub trending repositories, world population tables, documentation pages), paying particular attention to:

  • Locating relevant content with browser developer tools.

  • Designing CSS selectors or XPath expressions that are robust to small layout changes.

  • Respecting robots.txt, rate limits, and the website’s terms of service.

  • Avoiding brittle assumptions (e.g., exact tag order, class names) where possible.

1.2.4.2. Scraping full articles#

For many NLP tasks, titles alone are not enough. You may want full article text, author names, publication dates, and section headings. The typical workflow is:

  1. Scrape a listing page to collect links to individual articles.

  2. For each link:

    • Download the article page.

    • Identify the main content container (e.g., using <article> tags or heuristic rules).

    • Extract the text, removing boilerplate like navigation or footers.

  3. Store the results in a structured format (e.g., JSON or a database) with fields such as url, title, date, body, and tags.

Libraries like newspaper3k or trafilatura can automate parts of this process, but you should still inspect outputs and perform quality checks.

As a concrete example, you might build a corpus of flood-related news by scraping articles that mention “flood”, “river level”, or “evacuation” in headlines, then later use this corpus to study how risk communication evolves over time.

1.2.5. APIs and streaming data#

Some platforms explicitly expose their data via APIs rather than relying on scraping. For example:

  • Social platforms provide APIs for accessing posts, comments, and metadata (often with authentication and rate limits).

  • News APIs aggregate articles across sources and provide clean JSON responses.

  • Open-data APIs (e.g., for government data portals) expose structured data with textual fields (descriptions, titles, comments).

Working with APIs typically involves:

  • Registering for API keys and reading the platform’s terms of service.

  • Designing queries (e.g., keyword search, time windows, specific accounts).

  • Handling pagination and rate limiting.

  • Storing responses in a format that preserves both text and metadata.

While we do not dive deeply into specific APIs in this chapter, the same principles of responsible collection and documentation apply.

1.2.6. OCR and scanned documents#

Not all text is born digital. Reports, forms, books, and historical documents are often only available as scanned PDFs or images. Optical character recognition (OCR) bridges this gap by converting images of text into machine-readable strings.

The example below illustrates the core idea using Tesseract via the pytesseract Python wrapper. In practice, you will explore this more deeply in the OCR lab.

import pytesseract
from PIL import Image
from IPython.display import display

# -O plaid_c150.jpg ensures the file is overwritten if it already exists
!wget -q -O plaid_c150.jpg https://www.mattmahoney.net/ocr/plaid_c150.jpg

# Open and visualize the image before OCR
image = Image.open("plaid_c150.jpg")
print("Visualizing input image:")
display(image)

# Perform OCR
text = pytesseract.image_to_string(image, lang="eng")

print("-" * 20)
print("OCR Output:")
print(text[:500])

Visualizing input image:

--------------------
OCR Output:
CENTER
FOR THE i hisericdomiom Malioue

January 6-29

Fri & Saturdays at 8
Sandes Matinees at 2 pa

_ Tickets: 723-8698

| www.henegar.org pres rama tard

When using OCR in NLP workflows, be mindful of:

  • Document layout: multi-column text, tables, headers, and footnotes can confuse OCR engines.

  • Image quality: low resolution, poor contrast, skew, and noise all degrade accuracy.

  • Post-processing: spell correction, de-hyphenation, line joining, and paragraph segmentation can substantially improve downstream usability.

OCR is often the first step in building corpora from archival or domain-specific documents (e.g., environmental impact reports, scanned health records).

1.2.7. Open repositories with NLP-ready text#

Many institutions and companies host open datasets that already contain rich textual material. While the original motivation may not have been NLP, these resources can be repurposed for tasks such as classification, information extraction, or topic modeling.

1.2.7.1. Government and international organizations#

Many of the sources listed in other chapters continue to be relevant, but here we emphasize their potential for NLP:

  • Data.gov (United States)
    Thousands of datasets, including legislative documents, policy reports, and regulatory text. These can support tasks such as legal text classification, information extraction, and policy analysis.

  • U.S. Census Bureau
    Survey instruments, metadata, and technical documentation suitable for survey text analysis and question-answering tasks.

  • Open government portals (Canada, UK, Australia, and others)
    Often include reports, consultations, and official publications in addition to numeric indicators. These are rich sources for domain-specific language modeling.

1.2.7.2. Academic and research repositories#

Universities and research organizations frequently share not only numeric data but also textual artifacts such as survey instruments, interview transcripts, and article full texts.

  • UCI Machine Learning Repository
    Many datasets include textual fields (e.g., news headlines, reviews, abstracts) that can be used for classification and sentiment analysis.

  • Harvard Dataverse, Borealis, OSF, Figshare, Zenodo
    General-purpose repositories where researchers deposit data and accompanying documentation. Descriptions, codebooks, and narrative reports can serve as corpora for information extraction and summarization.

1.2.7.3. Technology platforms and NLP-specific collections#

Several technology companies and open-source communities maintain repositories specifically aimed at machine learning and NLP:

  • Hugging Face Datasets
    Large collection of ready-to-use NLP datasets (e.g., GLUE, SQuAD, IMDB, MultiNLI) accessible through the datasets Python library.

  • Google Dataset Search
    Meta-search engine for datasets across the web; useful for discovering corpora in specific domains.

  • AWS, Google Cloud, and Azure open-data programs
    Public datasets, including text corpora, that can be accessed directly from cloud environments for large-scale experiments.

In subsequent chapters, we will make use of several of these repositories and also discuss how to document your own datasets so they can be shared and reused by others.

1.2.8. Documenting datasets: dataset cards#

As corpora grow larger and more complex, it becomes increasingly important to document them. A common practice in the NLP community is to create a dataset card that describes:

  • The dataset’s purpose and intended use.

  • Data sources and collection methods.

  • Preprocessing steps and filtering criteria.

  • Known limitations, biases, and ethical considerations.

  • Licensing and citation instructions.

Thoughtful documentation not only supports reproducibility and responsible use, but also helps you reason about where your models might fail.

1.2.9. Ethics, licensing, and documentation#

Regardless of where your text data comes from, responsible NLP practice requires attention to:

  • Licensing and terms of use
    Ensure you have permission to collect, store, and analyze the text. Some sites prohibit scraping or restrict commercial use.

  • Privacy and consent
    Avoid collecting sensitive personal information without explicit consent and appropriate safeguards. Be especially careful with social media and logs.

  • Bias and representation
    Consider whose language is represented in your corpus and whose is missing. Data collection choices can systematically exclude certain communities or perspectives.

  • Documentation
    Record how the data was collected, cleaned, and filtered so that others (including your future self) can interpret your results correctly.

These issues become especially important when working with data scraped from the web or social media, or when using large, precompiled corpora to train or adapt language models.

1.2.10. Exercises and discussion#

  1. Corpus design from web sources
    Choose a topic you care about (e.g., wildfires, public health policy, renewable energy). Outline a plan for constructing a web-based corpus on this topic. Which sites would you target? What pages or sections would you scrape? What metadata would you store?

  2. Scraping ethics case study
    Consider a social media platform or online forum. Under what conditions is scraping content ethically acceptable? When might it be harmful? How would your answer change if you were building a public research dataset versus a commercial product?

  3. Dataset card sketch
    For the corpus you designed in Exercise 1, draft a one-page dataset card. Include sections on intended use, collection process, preprocessing, potential biases, and licensing.
