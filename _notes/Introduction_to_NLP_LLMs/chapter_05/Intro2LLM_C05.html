5. Inside Transformer LLMs and Text Classification#

With large language models introduced in Chapter 4, we are now ready to open the black box and examine the machinery that makes them work. This chapter explores the internal architecture of transformer LLMs in depth, examines how transformers process all tokens in parallel, and applies these foundations to the practical task of text classification.

We begin by looking inside a transformer LLM — tracing a complete forward pass from token IDs through the embedding layer, the 32-layer decoder stack, and the language model head to the final vocabulary distribution. We examine what logits are, how softmax converts them to probabilities, and how key-value caching makes autoregressive generation practical. We then study parallel token processing — the defining feature of transformers that distinguishes them from sequential recurrent models — and analyze how self-attention enables every token to simultaneously gather information from the full sequence. The second half of the chapter shifts to text classification, presenting six distinct approaches that span the full complexity spectrum from pre-trained classifiers to generative LLMs. We close with the practical question of how to leverage embeddings for classification and a survey of recent architectural improvements that have made modern LLMs more efficient and capable.

5.1. Learning objectives#

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • Inspect the full PyTorch module hierarchy of a transformer LLM and explain what each component (embedding layer, decoder layers, self-attention, MLP, LM head) does.

  • Trace a complete forward pass through a transformer decoder, interpreting the shape of each intermediate tensor.

  • Explain what logits are, how softmax converts them to a probability distribution, and how temperature reshapes that distribution.

  • Describe how key-value (KV) caching avoids redundant recomputation and quantify its impact on generation speed.

  • Explain how self-attention enables parallel processing of all tokens in a sequence and contrast this with recurrent models.

  • Describe the role of causal masking in autoregressive generation and how positional encoding (RoPE) encodes sequence order.

  • Apply six different text classification approaches — from pre-trained classifiers to generative LLMs — and compare them on accuracy, compute cost, and data requirements.

  • Use sentence embeddings for prototype-based and zero-shot classification without any labeled training data.

  • Fine-tune a RoBERTa model for sentiment classification and evaluate it using precision, recall, and F1-score.

  • Describe five architectural improvements in modern LLMs: pre-norm with RMSNorm, SwiGLU activation, Rotary Position Encoding (RoPE), weight tying, and grouped-query attention.

5.2. How this chapter fits into the course#

Chapter 4 introduced LLMs as black boxes — we loaded them, prompted them, and evaluated their outputs. This chapter opens the box.

Understanding the internal architecture connects forward to every advanced topic in the course:

  • Fine-tuning requires knowing which layers to freeze and which to update — knowledge that comes from understanding the module hierarchy and parameter distribution.

  • Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) relies on sentence embeddings introduced here for semantic search over document collections.

  • Prompt engineering benefits from understanding why the last-position logit determines the next token and how temperature controls generation diversity.

  • Efficient inference — serving models at scale — depends on KV caching and the architectural choices surveyed in Section 5.

The text classification material extends the supervised learning thread from Chapters 2 and 3 into the transformer era, connecting classical approaches (TF-IDF, Naive Bayes, SVM) to modern embedding-based and generative methods.

5.3. Chapter roadmap#

  1. Inside Transformer LLMs We inspect the full PyTorch architecture of Phi-3-Mini, trace a manual forward pass step by step, analyze the logit-to-probability conversion, extract top-k next token predictions, visualize the probability distribution at different temperatures, and measure the speedup from key-value caching.

  2. Parallel Token Processing We explain the core property that distinguishes transformers from recurrent models: every token is processed simultaneously through matrix multiplication rather than sequentially. We examine how self-attention computes queries, keys, and values in parallel, how causal masking enforces the autoregressive constraint, and how Rotary Position Encoding (RoPE) encodes positional information without sequential computation.

  3. Text Classification We implement and compare three generations of text classification approaches — classical sparse-feature pipelines (TF-IDF + SVM, TF-IDF + Naive Bayes), dense embedding networks (GloVe + neural network, GloVe + CNN), and fine-tuned transformers (RoBERTa) — on two benchmark datasets: Rotten Tomatoes movie reviews and Yelp polarity reviews. We evaluate robustness under negation, sarcasm, and cross-domain transfer.

  4. Text Classification with Leveraging Embeddings We explore embedding-based approaches to classification that require little or no labeled data: pre-trained sentiment classifiers (RoBERTa fine-tuned on Twitter), sentence embeddings with logistic regression, prototype/centroid classification, zero-shot embedding classification, generative classification with Flan-T5, and ChatGPT API classification. We compare all six approaches on accuracy, compute cost, latency, and data requirements.

  5. Recent Improvements to the Transformer Architecture We survey the key architectural changes that differentiate modern LLMs from the original transformer: pre-norm with RMSNorm, SwiGLU gated activation, Rotary Position Encoding, weight tying between the embedding and LM head, grouped-query attention (GQA), and flash attention. For each innovation we explain the original problem it solved and the empirical gains it delivered.
