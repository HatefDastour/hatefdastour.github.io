2. Part 2: Prompt Engineering Fundamentals#

2.1. What Is Prompt Engineering?#

Definition

The art and science of crafting effective instructions to maximize Large Language Model output quality.

The “Vague Prompt” Trap:

❌ Generic Prompt: “Write a summary”

  • Produces fluffy, generic, often inaccurate results

  • No clear parameters or constraints

  • Model makes arbitrary choices about length, style, focus

✅ Engineered Prompt: Uses structured frameworks to provide expertise, context, and methodological boundaries

Research Context

Studies show that well-engineered prompts can improve task performance by 20-50% compared to naive prompts, with even larger gains on complex reasoning tasks [Zhou et al., 2022].

2.2. The AIM Framework: Sharp Prompt Structure#

Moving beyond generic instructions, the AIM framework provides a systematic approach to prompt design [theMITmonk, 2023].

Components

  • Audience: Who will read/use this output?

  • Intent: What specific goal should the output achieve?

  • Methodology: How should the task be approached? What format, length, style?

Example 2.1 (Literature Review Analysis)

❌ Before (Generic):

"Summarize this paper on carbon sequestration."

✅ After (AIM Framework):

Audience: You are a senior research methodologist.

Intent: Here's an abstract from a recent paper on carbon sequestration: [paste abstract]. Identify the three most significant methodological limitations.

Methodology: Present as 3 bullet points, under 100 words total, using technical terminology appropriate for graduate-level environmental science researchers.

Result: Generic fluff → Sharp, usable output with specific, actionable insights.

Example 2.2 (Research Question Development)

❌ Before:

"Help me with my research question about social media."

✅ After (AIM Framework):

Audience: You are a research methods advisor for graduate students in communications.

Intent: I'm interested in studying "social media effects on mental health." Help me refine this into a focused research question.

Methodology: Think through this step-by-step:
1. Identify what's too vague (which social media? which mental health outcomes? which population?)
2. Narrow to one platform, one outcome, one demographic
3. Add a time dimension or comparative element
4. Propose 3 refined research questions ranked by feasibility for a master's thesis

Show your reasoning at each step.

2.3. Beyond AIM: The Research Prompting Landscape#

While AIM provides a solid foundation, different research tasks benefit from specialized frameworks.

Table 2.1 Framework Comparison#

Framework

Best For

Core Components

Use Case

ERA

Quick Tasks

Expectation, Role, Action

Fast literature summaries, quick classifications

CARE

Synthesizing Information

Context, Action, Result, Example

Combining multiple sources, pattern identification

ROSES

Strategic Planning

Role, Objective, Scenario, Expected Solution, Steps

Research design, methodology development

CREATE

Complex Writing/Drafting

Character, Request, Examples, Additions, Type, Extras

Grant proposals, manuscript drafts

Example 2.3 (ERA Framework (Expectation, Role, Action))

Expectation: I need a concise summary of research gaps in renewable energy policy.

Role: You are a policy analysis expert specializing in energy transitions.

Action: Review these three abstracts [paste] and identify 2-3 major research gaps, explaining why each matters for policy development. Format as bullet points, 150 words maximum.

Example 2.4 (CARE Framework (Context, Action, Result, Example))

Context: I'm conducting a meta-analysis of remote work productivity studies from 2020-2025.

Action: Extract key findings from this paper abstract [paste], focusing on quantitative productivity measures.

Result: I need a structured summary showing: (1) productivity metric used, (2) direction of effect, (3) effect size if reported, (4) study limitations.

Example: 
Paper X (2023): 
- Metric: Tasks completed per hour
- Effect: +15% increase
- Size: d = 0.42 (medium effect)
- Limitations: Self-reported data, tech workers only

Example 2.5 (ROSES Framework (Role, Objective, Scenario, Expected Solution, Steps))

Role: You are a research design consultant with expertise in mixed-methods studies.

Objective: Help me design a study examining factors influencing graduate student persistence in STEM programs.

Scenario: I have access to 5 years of enrollment data (n=2,000 students), can conduct 20-30 interviews, and have a 12-month timeline.

Expected Solution: A complete research design including:
- 2-3 specific research questions
- Mixed-methods approach justification
- Sampling strategy
- Analysis plan overview

Steps: First outline the research questions, then describe the quantitative phase, then the qualitative phase, finally explain how they integrate.

2.4. Core Principles of Effective Prompts#

Regardless of which framework you choose, certain principles consistently improve prompt effectiveness.

  • ❌ “Analyze this data”

  • ✅ “Calculate descriptive statistics (mean, SD, range) for variables X, Y, Z and identify any outliers beyond 3 SD from the mean”

  • Include relevant background

  • Specify length limits (word count, bullet points)

  • Define terminology when domain-specific

  • State any exclusions or boundaries

  • Ask model to “think step by step”

  • Request intermediate steps be shown

  • Improves accuracy on complex tasks by 30-50% [Kojima et al., 2022]

  • Show desired input-output patterns

  • 2-5 examples typically optimal

  • Improves pattern recognition and format consistency

  • Specify structure (bullet points, table, paragraph)

  • Request specific sections or headers

  • Define technical level and style

2.5. Group Activity: Framework Application#

Now it’s time to apply these concepts hands-on.

Exercise Instructions (12 minutes)

Setup: Form 4 groups, each assigned a framework (ERA, CARE, ROSES, CREATE)

Shared Scenario: “You’re preparing a research project idea for a grant application in your field.”

Group Tasks:

  1. Design one prompt using assigned framework (5 min)

  2. Run it in any LLM you have open (3 min)

  3. Record: One strength of output + one limitation/failure mode (2 min)

  4. One volunteer from group shares results (2 min total)

Expected Learning Outcomes:

  • Hands-on experience with structured prompting

  • Recognition that different frameworks suit different tasks

  • Understanding of framework limitations

  • Peer learning from comparing approaches

Debrief Questions:

  • Which framework felt most natural for this task?

  • What surprised you about the output?

  • How would you revise your prompt for a second attempt?

  • What did you notice from other groups’ results?
