Python Lab: Vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^n\)#

Ordered n-tuple#

An ordered sequence \((a_1 , a_2 , \ldots, a_n )\) of real numbers is called an ordered n-tuple. The word “ordered” here reflects our insistence that two ordered n-tuples are equal if and only if corresponding entries are the same. In other words, $\((a_1 , a_2 , \ldots, a_n ) = (b_1 , b_2 , \ldots, b_n )\)\( if and only if \)\(a_1 = b_1 , a_2 = b_2 , \ldots,\text{ and }a_n = b_n\)$

In python, we can define tuples similarly. For example,

A = (1,2,3)
type(A)

tuple

Throughout this article, we will be using many of the NumPy linear algebra functions.

Length of \(\overrightarrow{PQ}\)#

Let \(P = (p_1,\ldots,p_n)\) and \(Q=(q_1,\ldots,q_n)\) be two points in \(\mathbb{R}^n\). The distance between \(\vec{P}\) and \(\vec{Q}\) is the length of \(\overrightarrow{PQ}\),

\[\begin{align*} \|\overrightarrow{PQ}\|=\sqrt{(p_1-q_1)^2+(p_2-q_2)^2+(p_3-q_3)^2+\ldots+(p_n-q_n)^2} \end{align*}\]

Example: Let \(\vec{u}=\begin{bmatrix}1 & 2 & 2\end{bmatrix}^T\). Then the length of \(4\vec{u}=\begin{bmatrix}4 & 8 & 8\end{bmatrix}^T\) can be evaluated using

Let

import numpy as np
u = np.array([1, 2, 2])

To find the length of this vector, we can use norm

np.linalg.norm(u)

3.0

Moreover, the length of \(4\vec{u}\) is

np.linalg.norm(4*u)

12.0

The Dot Product#

Let \(\vec{u}=\begin{bmatrix} u_1 & \dots & u_n \end{bmatrix}^T\), \(\vec{v}=\begin{bmatrix} v_1 & \dots & v_n \end{bmatrix}^T\) be two vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^n\). Then, their dot product \(\vec{u} \bullet \vec{v}\) is a number defined as $\(\begin{aligned} \vec{u} \bullet \vec{v}=\begin{bmatrix} u_1 \\ \vdots \\ u_n \end{bmatrix} \bullet \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ \vdots \\ v_n \end{bmatrix}=u_1v_1+u_2v_2+\ldots+u_nv_n.\end{aligned}\)$

Example: Find \(\vec{u} \bullet \vec{v}\) if \(\vec{u} =\begin{bmatrix} 3 & 13 & 3 \end{bmatrix}^T\) and \(\vec{v} =\begin{bmatrix} 17 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}^T\)

This time, we can use vdot

u = np.array([3, 13, 3])
v = np.array([17, 0, -1])
np.vdot(u, v)

48

We also could use dotor inner as well

np.dot(u,v)

48

np.inner(u,v)

48

The Cross Product#

The cross product of the special vectors \(\vec{i}\), \(\vec{j}\) and \(\vec{k}\) is as follows. $\(\begin{aligned} \begin{array}{cc} \vec{i}\times \vec{j} = \vec{k} & \vec{j}\times \vec{i} = - \vec{k} \\ \vec{k} \times \vec{i} = \vec{j} & \vec{i}\times \vec{k} = - \vec{j} \\ \vec{j}\times \vec{k} = \vec{i} & \vec{k}\times \vec{j} = - \vec{i} \\ \end{array}\end{aligned}\)$


Let \(\vec{u}= u_1 \vec{i}+u_2\vec{j}+u_3 \vec{k}\) and \(\vec{v} = v_1 \vec{i}+v_2\vec{j}+v_3\vec{k}\) be two vectors. Then $\(\begin{aligned} \vec{u} \times \vec{v} &=\begin{bmatrix}u_2 v_3 -u_3 v_2\\-(u_1v_3 -u_3 v_1 )\\u_1 v_2 -u_2 v_1\end{bmatrix}\\ &=(u_2 v_3 -u_3 v_2)\vec{i}-(u_1v_3 -u_3 v_1 )\vec{j}+(u_1 v_2 -u_2 v_1)\vec{k}\end{aligned}\)$

Example: Find \(\vec{u} \times \vec{v}\) if \(\vec{u} =\begin{bmatrix} 3 & 13 & 3 \end{bmatrix}^T\) and \(\vec{v} =\begin{bmatrix} 17 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}^T\)

We have

u = np.array([3, 13, 3])
v = np.array([17, 0, -1])
np.cross(u, v)

array([ -13,   54, -221])
