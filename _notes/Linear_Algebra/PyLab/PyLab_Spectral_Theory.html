9.9.1. Eigenvectors and Eigenvalues#

Let \(A\) be an \(n\times n\) matrix and let \(X \in \mathbb{C}^n\) be a nonzero vector for which

\[\begin{align*} AX = \lambda X \end{align*}\]

for some scalar \(\lambda\) . Then \(\lambda\) is called an eigenvalue of the matrix \(A\) and \(X\) is called an eigenvector of \(A\) associated with \(\lambda\), or a \(\lambda\)-eigenvector of \(A\).

Example: Find the eigenvalues of \(A=\left[\begin{array}{ccc} -2 & -2 & -9\\ -1 & 1 & -3\\ 1 & 1 & 4 \end{array}\right]\)

Here we use two methods. The first method is to use the Matrix.

from __future__ import division
from sympy import init_session, init_printing, Matrix
init_printing(use_unicode=True)

A = Matrix([[-2, -2, -9], [-1, 1, -3], [1, 1, 4]])
A

from sympy import symbols, factor
lamda = symbols('lamda')
p = A.charpoly(lamda)
factor(p.as_expr())

A.eigenvals()

The second method is to use arrays and linalg from Numpy. This requires converting the Matrix A into an array.

import numpy as np
eigenvectors,_ = np.linalg.eig(np.asarray(A, dtype = int))
eigenvectors

array([1.+1.j, 1.-1.j, 1.+0.j])

9.9.2. Finding Eigenvectors and Eigenvalues#

Let \(A\) be an \(n\times n\) matrix.

  1. The eigenvalues of \(A\) are the roots of \(c_A(x)\).

  2. The \(\lambda\)-eigenvectors \(X\) are the nontrivial solutions to \((\lambda I-A)X=0\).

Example: Find the characteristic polynomial, eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the matrix $\(\begin{aligned} A=\left[\begin{array}{rrr} 4 & 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 3 & -2 \\ 0 & -1 & 2 \end{array}\right].\end{aligned}\)$

A = Matrix([[4, 1, 2], [0, 3, -2], [0, -1, 2]])

lamda = symbols('lamda')
p = A.charpoly(lamda)
factor(p.as_expr())

A.eigenvals()

A.eigenvects()

Alternatively,

eigenvalues, eigenvectors = np.linalg.eig(np.asarray(A, dtype = int))

        [ 0.        , -0.4472136 ,  0.57735027]]))

9.9.3. Diagonalizing a Matrix#

Let \(A\) be an \(n\times n\) matrix. The process of finding an invertible matrix \(P\) and a diagonal matrix \(D\) so that \(A=PDP^{-1}\) is referred to as diagonalizing the matrix \(A\), and \(P\) is called the diagonalizing matrix for \(A\).

The key to diagonalizing a matrix (finding the matrices \(P\) and \(D\)) lies in the eigenvectors and eigenvalues of the matrix \(A\).

9.9.3.1. Eigenvectors and Diagonalizable Matrices#

Let \(A\) be an \(n\times n\) matrix.

  1. \(A\) is diagonalizable if and only if it has eigenvectors \(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n\) so that \(P = \left[\begin{array}{cccc} X_1 & X_2 & \cdots & X_n \end{array}\right]\) is invertible.

  2. If \(P\) is invertible, then $\(P^{-1}AP=\mathrm{Diag}(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \ldots, \lambda_n )\)\( where \)\lambda_i\( is the eigenvalue of \)A\( corresponding to the eigenvector \)X_i\(, i.e., \)AX_i=\lambda_i X_i$.

Example: Let \(A =\left[\begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 0 & 0\\ 0 & 2 & -1\\ 0 & 1 & 2 \end{array}\right]\). Find an invertible matrix \(P\) and a diagonal matrix \(D\) such that \(P^{-1}AP = D\).

A = Matrix([[1, 0, 0], [0, 2, -1], [0, 1 ,2]])
A

lamda = symbols('lamda')
p = A.charpoly(lamda)
factor(p.as_expr())

The eigenvalues of \(A\) are the the roots of \(c_A(\lambda)\):

A.eigenvals()

The correcponding eigenvectors:

A.eigenvects()

diagonalizing matrix for \(A\)

P, D=A.diagonalize()

D

P**-1

We can verify our results

9.9.4. Raising a Matrix to a High Power#

Suppose we have a matrix \(A\) and we want to find \(A^{20}\). You could try to multiply A with itself 20 times; however, this is computationally extremely intensive and it is not recommended. We also can diagonalization to compute high powers of a matrix relatively easily.

Suppose \(A\) is diagonalizable, so there is an invertible matrix \(P\) such that \(P^{-1} AP = D\). We can rearrange this equation to $\(\begin{aligned} &P^{-1} AP = D,\\ \Rightarrow\quad & PP^{-1} AP = PD,\\ \Rightarrow \quad & AP=PD,\\ \Rightarrow\quad & APP^{-1}=PDP^{-1},\\ \Rightarrow \quad & A=PDP^{-1}.\end{aligned}\)\( Now, consider \)A^2\(. Since \)A=PDP^{-1}\(, it follows that \)\(\begin{aligned} A^2=\underbrace{PDP^{-1}}_{A}\underbrace{PDP^{-1}}_{A}=PD^2P^{-1}.\end{aligned}\)\( Similarly, \)\(\begin{aligned} A^3=\underbrace{PDP^{-1}}_{A}\underbrace{PDP^{-1}}_{A}\underbrace{PDP^{-1}}_{A}=PD^3P^{-1}.\end{aligned}\)\( In general, \)\(\begin{aligned} A^n=\underbrace{PDP^{-1}PDP^{-1}\ldots PDP^{-1}}_{n\text{ times}}=PD^nP^{-1}.\end{aligned}\)\( Therefore, we have reduced the problem to finding \)D^n\(. In order to compute \)D^n\( , then because \)D\( is diagonal we only need to raise every entry on the main diagonal of \)D\( to the power of \)n$.

Example: Let \(A =\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 0\\ 0 & 2 & 0\\ 0 & 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\). Find \(A^{10}\).

A = Matrix([[1, 1, 0],[ 0, 2, 0],[ 0, 2, 1]])
A

lamda = symbols('lamda')
p = A.charpoly(lamda)
factor(p.as_expr())

The eigenvalues of \(A\) are the the roots of \(c_A(\lambda)\):

A.eigenvals()

The correcponding eigenvectors:

A.eigenvects()

diagonalizing matrix for \(A\)

P, D=A.diagonalize()
P

D

P**-1

We can verify our results

simplify(P*D*P**-1)

Therefore, \(A^{10}\)

P*(D**(10))*P**-1

n = symbols('n')
P*(D**(n))*P**-1

9.9.5. Orthogonal Matrices#

\(A\) real \(n\times n\) matrix \(U\) is called an orthogonal matrix if \(UU^T =U^T U = I\). \end{definition}

Example: Show the matrix \(U =\left[\begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 0 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & -1\\ 0 & -1 & 0 \end{array}\right]\) is orthogonal.

U=Matrix([[1,0,0],[0,0,-1],[0,-1,0]])
U

U*U**-1

U**-1*U

Hence, this matrix is orthogonal

9.9.6. Determinant of Orthogonal Matrices#

Suppose \(U\) is an orthogonal matrix. Then, \(det(U) = 1\) or \(det(U) = -1\).

Example

U=Matrix([[1,0,0],[0,0,-1],[0,-1,0]])
U

\(det(U)\)=

from sympy import det
det(U)

We know that a matrix \(A\) is called symmetric if \(A = A^T\). If the matrix \(A\) is symmetric, then we can actually find such a matrix \(P\) that is an orthogonal matrix of eigenvectors. Thus \(P^{-1}\) is simply its transpose \(P^T\), and \(P^T AP\) is diagonal. When this happens we say that \(A\) is orthogonally diagonalizable.

An \(n\times n\) matrix \(A\) is said to be orthogonally diagonalizable when an orthogonal matrix \(P\) can be found such that \(P^{-1} AP = P^T AP\) is diagonal.

Example: Find an orthogonal matrix \(P\) such that \(P^{T} AP\) is diagonal, where \(A = \left[\begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 0 & 0\\ 0 & 3 & 1\\ 0 & 1 & 3 \end{array}\right]\).

A = Matrix([[1,0,0],[0,3,1],[0,1,3]])
A

lamda = symbols('lamda')
p = A.charpoly(lamda)
factor(p)
