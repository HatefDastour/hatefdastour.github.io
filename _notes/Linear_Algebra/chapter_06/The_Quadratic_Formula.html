The Quadratic Formula

The Quadratic Formula#

Observe that, on one hand, we have \(i^2=-1\); on the other hand, \((-i)^2=i^2=-1\). In other words, both \(i\) and \(-i\) are the square roots of \(-1\). \(i\) is sometimes called the principal square root of \(-1\), and for a positive number \(c\), we have,

\[\begin{align*} \sqrt{-c}=\sqrt{c}i \end{align*}\]

Therefore, the roots of the quadratic equation \(ax^2+bx+c=0\) are still valid even for the case that \(b^2-4ac<0\),

\[\begin{align*} x=\frac{-b\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} =\left\{ \begin{array}{l} \dfrac{-b+\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \\ \dfrac{-b-\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \end{array}\right. \end{align*}\]

  • \(b^2-4ac\geq 0\): the roots of the quadratic are real;

  • \(b^2-4ac<0\): the quadratic has no real roots.

Example: Find the roots of the equation \(x^2+x+1=0\).

Solution: Using the quadratic formula, we have

\[\begin{align*} \text{The roots }&=\frac{-1\pm\sqrt{1^2-4(1)(1)}}{2(1)}=\frac{-1\pm\sqrt{1-4}}{2}=\frac{-1\pm\sqrt{3}i}{2}\\ &=\begin{cases} \dfrac{-1+\sqrt{3}i}{2} \\ \dfrac{-1-\sqrt{3}i}{2} \end{cases} =\begin{cases} -\dfrac{1}{2}-\dfrac{\sqrt{3}\,i}{2} \\ -\dfrac{1}{2}+\dfrac{\sqrt{3}\,i}{2} \end{cases} \end{align*}\]
Theorem: The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra

Every positive degree polynomial with complex coefficients has a complex root.

Example: Find the roots of the quadratic \(x^2-(3-2i)x+(5-i)=0\).

Solution: Using the quadratic formula

\[\begin{align*} x=\frac{(3-2i) \pm\sqrt{(-(3-2i))^2-4(5-i)}}{2}. \end{align*}\]

Note that

\[\begin{align*} (-(3-2i))^2-4(5-i) = 5-12i-20+4i=-15-8i,\end{align*}\]

Hence,

\[\begin{align*} x=\frac{3-2i \pm\sqrt{-15-8i}}{2}.\end{align*}\]

To find \(\pm\sqrt{-15-8i}\), solve \(z^2=-15-8i\) for \(z\). Let \(z=a+bi\) and \(z^2=-15-8i\).

Then

\[\begin{align*} &(a^2-b^2)+2abi=-15-8i, \quad \Rightarrow \quad \begin{cases} a^2-b^2=-15, \\ 2ab=-8. \end{cases} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \begin{cases} a^2-b^2=-15, \\ a=-4/b. \end{cases} \end{align*}\]

Solving for \(a\) and \(b\)

\[\begin{align*} \left(-\frac{4}{b}\right)^2-b^2=-15 \quad \Rightarrow \quad (b-4) (b+4) \underbrace{(b^2+1)}_{\text{No real roots!}}=0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad b=\pm 4\\ \text{Since }a=-4/b \Rightarrow \quad a=\mp 1. \end{align*}\]

Therefore, \(z= 1-4i, -1+4i\), i.e., \(z=\pm(1-4i)\). We have,

\[\begin{align*} x=\frac{(3-2i) \pm(1-4i)}{2}= \begin{cases} \dfrac{(3-2i) +(1-4i)}{2} =\dfrac{4-6i}{2} = 2-3i, \\ \dfrac{(3-2i) -(1-4i)}{2} =\dfrac{2+2i}{2}= 1+i. \end{cases}\end{align*}\]

Thus, the roots of \(x^2-(3-2i)x+(5-i)\) are \(2-3i\) and \(1+i\).
